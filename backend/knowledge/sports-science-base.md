## READINESS: HRV as a Recovery Marker in Youth Athletes
rec_types: [READINESS, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) reflects parasympathetic nervous system activity and is the gold-standard non-invasive recovery marker. In youth athletes, HRV baseline should be established over 7-14 days of morning measurements. A drop >15% below individual baseline indicates parasympathetic suppression — the body is still recovering. Training intensity should be reduced to RPE 5-6 (zone 1-2) until HRV returns within 1 SD of baseline. Important: HRV is highly individual — absolute values are meaningless; only RELATIVE change from personal baseline matters. Day-to-day variation of 10-15% is normal. Trends over 3-5 days are more reliable than single readings.

## READINESS: RED Readiness Flag — When to Modify Training
rec_types: [READINESS]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

A RED readiness flag indicates multiple recovery signals are suppressed: elevated resting HR (>10% above baseline), depressed HRV, poor sleep quality (<6hrs or fragmented), high soreness (≥8/10), or pain reported. Under RED status: (1) No high-intensity training (>RPE 7), (2) No maximal-effort testing, (3) No plyometrics or heavy eccentric loading, (4) Light technical work, mobility, or active recovery ONLY. Attempting high-intensity training under RED increases injury risk by 2-3x (Gabbett 2016). The athlete should NOT feel guilty about modifying — recovery IS training. Return to full intensity only when readiness returns to AMBER or GREEN for 2 consecutive days.

## READINESS: Sleep Quality and Athletic Performance
rec_types: [READINESS, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Youth athletes require 8-10 hours of sleep for optimal recovery and adaptation. Sleep <7hrs reduces reaction time by 20%, increases injury risk by 1.7x, and impairs muscle protein synthesis by up to 30%. Sleep quality markers: (1) Sleep onset <20min, (2) No more than 1 awakening, (3) Time in bed efficiency >85%. For match-day preparation: target 9hrs the TWO nights before (not just the night before). Screen time cutoff 60min before bed. Post-training evening: light stretching + breathing exercises promote parasympathetic activation and faster sleep onset.

## LOAD_WARNING: ACWR and Injury Risk in Youth Sport
rec_types: [LOAD_WARNING]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

The Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (ACWR) compares recent load (7-day) to established fitness (28-day rolling average). The "sweet spot" for injury prevention is ACWR 0.80-1.30. Below 0.80 = undertrained (fitness declining). Above 1.30 = spike zone (injury risk increases exponentially). Above 1.50 = danger zone (2-4x injury risk). Important: Use the exponentially weighted moving average (EWMA) method, not simple rolling average, as it's more sensitive to recent changes. For youth athletes, be MORE conservative: sweet spot 0.85-1.20. Week-to-week load increases should not exceed 10% (acute spikes beyond this = injury risk).

## LOAD_WARNING: Training Load Spikes and Deload Protocols
rec_types: [LOAD_WARNING, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

When ACWR exceeds 1.30 or weekly load increases >15%, a structured deload is required. Deload options: (1) Volume reduction: maintain intensity, reduce total sets/reps/duration by 40-50% for 3-5 days. (2) Intensity reduction: maintain volume, reduce RPE to 5-6 for 3-5 days. (3) Active rest: low-intensity movement only (walking, swimming, yoga) for 2-3 days. The deload is NOT optional — it prevents overreaching from becoming overtraining syndrome (which can take 6-12 weeks to resolve vs 3-5 days of proactive deload). Post-deload: resume at 85-90% of pre-spike load and build gradually.

## RECOVERY: Active Recovery Protocols for Youth Athletes
rec_types: [RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Active recovery (light movement at HR zone 1, 50-60% max HR) within 24-48h post-match reduces delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) by 15-20% compared to complete rest. Protocol: 15-20min light cycling, swimming, or walking + 10-15min dynamic stretching + foam rolling on major muscle groups. Timing: Day+1 post-match or post-high-intensity session. Avoid: static stretching immediately post-exercise (reduces force output for 24hrs). Cold water immersion (10-12°C, 10-15min) has moderate evidence for reducing inflammation but may blunt training adaptations if used routinely — reserve for tournament/congested fixtures only.

## RECOVERY: Post-Match Recovery Timeline
rec_types: [RECOVERY, READINESS]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Match recovery follows a predictable timeline: 0-2hrs: refuel (1.0-1.2g carbohydrate/kg + 0.3g protein/kg within 30min). 2-12hrs: hydration (replace 150% of fluid lost). 12-24hrs: muscle damage peaks (CK levels highest). 24-48hrs: neuromuscular fatigue persists (reduce power output). 48-72hrs: full neuromuscular recovery for most youth athletes. Training implications: Day+1 = active recovery or rest ONLY. Day+2 = light technical, no high-intensity. Day+3 = return to normal training if readiness signals are GREEN. For congested fixture periods (2+ matches/week): prioritize sleep and nutrition over additional training.

## DEVELOPMENT: Speed Development Windows in Youth Athletes
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Speed development has two primary windows: (1) Pre-PHV (ages 7-12): focus on agility, coordination, change of direction, neural pathway development. Short sprints (<30m), multi-directional. Avoid heavy resistance training. (2) Post-PHV (ages 15+): neuromuscular maturity allows maximal sprint training, plyometrics, and strength-speed work. During PHV (Circa-PHV): CAUTION — rapid limb growth disrupts coordination. Focus on technique maintenance, not PBs. Reduce plyometric volume by 50%. Avoid heavy eccentric loading. Sprint mechanics work (A-skips, B-skips) is safe and beneficial. Key metric: 10m sprint time reflects acceleration ability (most sport-relevant). 30m reflects max velocity capacity.

## DEVELOPMENT: Strength Training Progression for Youth Athletes
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Youth strength training is safe and effective when properly progressed. Stages: (1) Movement competency (all ages): bodyweight patterns — squat, lunge, push-up, plank, hinge. Master form before adding load. (2) Load introduction (post-movement competency, typically U14+): light external load, 2-3 sets × 8-12 reps, 2x/week. Focus on rate of force development, not max load. (3) Strength building (post-PHV, typically U16+): progressive overload, 3-4 sets × 6-10 reps, compound lifts (squat, deadlift, bench). Weekly load increase ≤5%. Never train to absolute failure in youth athletes — leave 2-3 reps in reserve. Circa-PHV: reduce loading by 40%, maintain movement patterns, monitor for apophyseal pain (growth plate stress).

## DEVELOPMENT: Periodization for In-Season Youth Athletes
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT, LOAD_WARNING]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

In-season training must balance competition readiness with development. Weekly structure: Match-day minus 3 (MD-3): highest intensity training day (speed, power, tactical). MD-2: moderate intensity (technical, position-specific). MD-1: activation only (short, sharp, 30min max). MD+1: recovery/rest. MD+2: return to training (moderate). This creates a natural undulating load pattern that prevents ACWR spikes. In-season strength training: maintain 2x/week, reduce volume by 30% from off-season, maintain intensity. Priority: do NOT add training volume during congested periods — subtract. Quality > quantity.

## ACADEMIC: Managing Dual Load — School + Sport Stress
rec_types: [ACADEMIC]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

Academic and athletic stress share the same cortisol pathway. Exam stress + heavy training = compounded fatigue. Research shows: (1) cognitive fatigue before physical training reduces performance by 10-15%, (2) exam-week cortisol levels can match match-day levels, (3) sleep disruption from academic stress directly impairs physical recovery. Recommendations: During exam periods, reduce training volume by 30-40% but maintain some physical activity (reduces cortisol vs complete rest). Schedule study sessions 2+ hours before training. Use the "study-train-sleep" sequence (not "train-study") for optimal cognitive and physical outcomes. Academic load should be factored into total athlete load monitoring.

## ACADEMIC: Exam Period Training Modifications
rec_types: [ACADEMIC, READINESS]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

During exam periods (2+ exams within 7 days): reduce training to 60-70% of normal volume. Maintain 2-3 sessions/week but cap at 45-60min. Focus on enjoyment and stress relief rather than performance gains. Light aerobic activity (20-30min zone 1-2) before study sessions improves cognitive function by 15-20% (Hillman et al., 2008). Avoid: scheduling high-intensity training the evening before an exam (elevates cortisol, disrupts sleep). Study plan + training plan should be coordinated — Tomo's schedule engine accounts for exam proximity in the priority matrix. Post-exam: gradual return to full load over 5-7 days (avoid the "make up for lost time" spike).

## PHV: Training During Peak Height Velocity
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT, LOAD_WARNING]
phv_stages: [CIRCA]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Peak Height Velocity (PHV) is the period of fastest growth (typically 12-14 for boys, 10-12 for girls). During Circa-PHV: bones grow faster than muscles and tendons, creating vulnerability. Key risks: Osgood-Schlatter (knee), Sever's disease (heel), apophyseal avulsion fractures. Training modifications: (1) Reduce plyometric volume by 50%, (2) Reduce eccentric loading intensity, (3) No maximal 1RM testing, (4) Extra stretching for hip flexors, hamstrings, calves (tightest during growth), (5) Loading multiplier: 0.60× normal training load. Monitor: any new joint pain = stop activity and assess. Coordination may temporarily regress ("adolescent awkwardness") — this is NORMAL, not a skill deficit. Maintain technical practice but don't expect PBs.

## PHV: Growth-Related Injury Prevention
rec_types: [LOAD_WARNING]
phv_stages: [CIRCA]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Growth plate (physis) injuries account for 15-30% of youth sport injuries during PHV. Risk factors: rapid growth (>6cm in 6 months), high training volume, sport specialization. Prevention: (1) Nordic hamstring exercises 2x/week (reduce hamstring strain risk by 51%), (2) Balance and proprioception training (reduces ankle injury by 40%), (3) Hip and core stability work (reduces knee valgus), (4) Progressive return from any growth-related pain (minimum 2 pain-free days before return). Red flags requiring medical clearance: pain at rest, night pain, joint swelling, loss of range of motion. NEVER train through growth plate pain — the risk of permanent growth arrest is real.

## INJURY_PREVENTION: Pain Flagging and Training Decisions
rec_types: [READINESS, LOAD_WARNING]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

When an athlete reports pain in their check-in, follow this decision tree: (1) Pain at rest or night pain = DO NOT TRAIN, seek medical assessment within 48hrs. (2) Pain during warm-up that worsens = STOP SESSION, active recovery only. (3) Pain during warm-up that resolves = MODIFY — reduce intensity to RPE 5-6, avoid pain-provoking movements. (4) Pain only after training = MONITOR — log pain location and severity, modify next session if recurring. (5) Pain >3 days = REST + professional assessment. Critical: soreness ≠ pain. Soreness (bilateral, muscle belly, resolves with warm-up) is normal. Pain (unilateral, joint/tendon, worsens with activity) is a warning signal. Youth athletes often underreport pain — always ask specifically.

## INJURY_PREVENTION: Return-to-Play Criteria
rec_types: [READINESS, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Return-to-play after injury follows a graduated protocol: Stage 1 (pain-free daily activities), Stage 2 (light aerobic, HR <70% max), Stage 3 (sport-specific drills, no contact), Stage 4 (full training with contact), Stage 5 (match clearance). Minimum 2 days at each stage before progressing. Regression criteria: any return of pain = drop back one stage. For muscular injuries: strength must be within 10% of uninjured side. For joint injuries: full range of motion required before Stage 3. Psychological readiness matters — fear of re-injury is real and should be acknowledged, not dismissed. Use confidence scaling (1-10) alongside physical markers.

## MOTIVATION: Self-Determination Theory in Youth Sport
rec_types: [MOTIVATION]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Intrinsic motivation (playing because you enjoy it) is the strongest predictor of long-term sport participation. Three basic psychological needs (Deci & Ryan): (1) Autonomy — choice in training decisions, goal-setting involvement. (2) Competence — appropriate challenge level, progress visibility, mastery experiences. (3) Relatedness — belonging, team connection, coach relationship. When these needs are met, athletes show higher effort, persistence, and well-being. When frustrated: burnout, dropout, anxiety. Coaching implications: offer choices within structure ("which drill set do you want to start with?"), celebrate process goals alongside outcome goals, never use training as punishment.

## MOTIVATION: Streak Psychology and Habit Formation
rec_types: [MOTIVATION]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

Consistency streaks leverage "loss aversion" — athletes are more motivated to maintain a streak than to start one. Research shows: 21 days creates a habit, 66 days makes it automatic (Lally et al., 2010). Design principles: (1) Make the minimum daily action EASY (a check-in counts), (2) Allow "freeze days" to prevent all-or-nothing abandonment, (3) Show streak milestones at 7, 14, 21, 30, 60, 90 days, (4) Social comparison (leaderboards) motivates competitive athletes but can demotivate lower performers — use with care. Key: the goal is ENGAGEMENT, not perfection. A 5-day streak with genuine effort > a 30-day streak of minimum-effort check-ins.

## NUTRITION: Youth Athlete Fueling Basics
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Youth athletes have higher metabolic rates per kg than adults. Daily energy needs: 2,500-4,000 kcal depending on age, size, and training load. Key principles: (1) Carbohydrate is the primary fuel for sport — 5-7g/kg/day for moderate training, 7-10g/kg/day for heavy training days. (2) Protein for recovery: 1.2-1.6g/kg/day, spread across 4+ meals/snacks (max 0.4g/kg per sitting). (3) Pre-training meal: 2-3hrs before, carb-rich, low fat. (4) During training >60min: 30-60g carbs/hr (sports drink or fruit). (5) Post-training: carbs + protein within 30-60min. NEVER restrict calories in growing athletes. Avoid supplements — food first. Creatine only post-PHV with professional guidance.

## NUTRITION: Match-Day and Competition Nutrition
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Match-day nutrition timeline: Night before: carb-loading meal (pasta, rice, potatoes) + good hydration. Morning of: familiar breakfast 3-4hrs before kickoff (toast, cereal, fruit, yogurt). 1hr before: light snack if needed (banana, energy bar). During: water + electrolytes every 15-20min. Halftime: 200-300ml fluid + small carb snack. Post-match: recovery meal within 60min (carbs + protein). Common mistakes: trying new foods on match day, caffeine use in U16 athletes (not recommended), skipping breakfast for early matches. Hydration check: urine should be pale yellow. Dark urine = inadequate hydration.

Football talent identification uses multi-dimensional profiling. ## READINESS: Resting Heart Rate as a Recovery Indicator
rec_types: [READINESS]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Resting heart rate (RHR) is a simple but effective recovery marker when tracked consistently. Normal youth athlete RHR: 55-75 bpm (lower indicates better cardiovascular fitness). A rise of >5bpm above individual baseline suggests: inadequate recovery, illness onset, psychological stress, or overtraining. Measure: morning, before getting out of bed, after 2min of quiet lying. Factors that elevate RHR: caffeine, dehydration, poor sleep, illness, high training load. RHR combined with HRV provides a more complete picture of autonomic nervous system status. Trend over 7 days is more informative than single readings.

age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]

Physical recovery without mental recovery is incomplete. Psychological load sources: competition pressure, selection anxiety, academic stress, social media comparison, coach conflict. age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [padel]

Padel-specific development: (1) Lateral movement — court is 10m wide, constant side-to-side. Train with lateral shuffle drills, split-step timing, deceleration mechanics. ## DEVELOPMENT: Athletics Sprint Event Training
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [athletics]
evidence_grade: A

Sprint event training (100m/200m/400m): (1) Phase model — acceleration (0-30m), max velocity (30-60m), speed maintenance (60-100m). Each requires specific training. (2) Block starts — 3x/week during competition prep, focus on first-step mechanics and drive phase angle. (3) Max velocity — flying sprints (30m fly-in), wicket runs for stride frequency. Key metrics: 10m (acceleration), 30m (transition), flying 20m (max velocity), 60m (competition predictor).

## LOAD_MANAGEMENT: Football Match-Day Load Management
rec_types: [READINESS, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football]
evidence_grade: A

Football match-day load management centers on the Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (ACWR) framework (Gabbett 2016). The optimal ACWR "sweet spot" for injury prevention is 0.8–1.3; ratios above 1.5 significantly increase injury risk (by 2–4x). For match-day minus protocols: MD-3 should be the highest training load day (~80% of weekly peak), MD-2 moderate tactical work (~60%), MD-1 activation only (~30%), focusing on set pieces, short sharp sprints under 20m, and tactical walkthroughs. Post-match: MD+1 is active recovery (pool, bike, 15–20 min at <65% HRmax), MD+2 light technical with zero high-speed running (>5.5 m/s). Key GPS metrics to track weekly: total distance (youth U17 target 5,000–6,500m/match), high-speed running distance (>5.5 m/s, typically 400–800m for youth), sprint distance (>7 m/s, typically 150–350m), accelerations/decelerations (>3 m/s², 40–60 per match). Weekly training load should build chronic load gradually — no more than 10% week-on-week increase. During congested fixture periods (2 matches/week), reduce total training volume by 30–40% but maintain intensity of remaining sessions. Monitor session RPE (sRPE = RPE × duration) daily; weekly sRPE targets: U15 2,500–3,500 AU, U17 3,000–4,500 AU, U19 3,500–5,500 AU. Players returning from injury should follow a graded ACWR build: week 1 target 0.6, week 2 target 0.8, week 3 target 1.0, only cleared for full match when ACWR is stable at 0.8–1.2 for 2+ weeks.

## LOAD_MANAGEMENT: Football Pre-Season Loading Framework

phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football]
evidence_grade: A

Football pre-season typically spans 4–6 weeks and follows a progressive overload model. Block 1 (weeks 1–2): Aerobic foundation — 70–80% of sessions target aerobic development. Total weekly distance should start at 60% of in-season average and progress to 80% by end of block. Include 2–3 long-duration intermittent runs (Yo-Yo style intervals, 15/15 or 30/15 protocols). ## LOAD_MANAGEMENT: Padel Wrist and Shoulder Overuse Prevention
rec_types: [RECOVERY, DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [padel]
evidence_grade: B+

Padel players face unique upper-limb overuse risks due to the solid racket (no string dampening), wall rebounds creating unexpected impact forces, and the high volume of overhead smashes (bandeja, vibora). Wrist injuries account for approximately 15–20% of padel injuries, with extensor tendinopathy and TFCC (triangular fibrocartilage complex) lesions being most common. The overhead smash is the highest-risk stroke — limit smash practice to 50–80 repetitions per session for youth, with 48-hour recovery before the next heavy overhead session. (4) Volume management — weekly hitting volume should not exceed: U13: 5–6 hours, U15: 7–9 hours, U17: 10–12 hours, U19+: 12–15 hours. Track weekly smash count; a sudden increase >30% correlates with shoulder and wrist complaints. If pain rated >3/10 on VAS during play, stop immediately and apply POLICE protocol (Protection, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation). Return to play requires pain-free overhead hitting for 3 consecutive sessions.

## LOAD_MANAGEMENT: Athletics Sprint Session CNS Recovery
rec_types: [READINESS, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [athletics]
evidence_grade: A

Sprint training places the highest demand on the central nervous system (CNS) of any training modality. Maximum-velocity sprinting (95–100% effort, distances 30–80m) requires 48–72 hours of CNS recovery in trained athletes, and potentially longer (72–96 hours) in developing youth athletes (Haugen 2019). CNS fatigue manifests as: reduced reaction time, decreased rate of force development, subjective "heaviness" in legs despite no muscular soreness, poor coordination in technical drills. Session design for CNS management: (1) Volume limits — total maximal sprint volume per session should not exceed: U15: 200–300m, U17: 300–400m, U19: 400–600m. This is TOTAL volume at 95%+ intensity, not including warm-up runs. (2) Recovery between reps — full recovery is essential: 1 minute per 10m sprinted (e.g., 60m sprint = 6 minutes rest). Incomplete recovery shifts the training stimulus from speed/neural to speed endurance, which is a different adaptation. (3) Weekly structure — maximum of 2 high-CNS sessions per week for youth, separated by at least 48 hours. Between CNS-intensive days, use tempo runs (65–75% effort, 100–200m reps with 60–90s rest) which are regenerative, not fatiguing. (4) CNS monitoring — simple tests: grip dynamometer (decline >10% from baseline = CNS fatigue), 10m fly time (decline >3%), reactive strength index from drop jump (decline >8%). If CNS markers are suppressed, convert planned speed session to tempo or technical work. (5) Complementary training on CNS recovery days: light plyometrics (<40 ground contacts), mobility work, low-intensity strength (50–60% 1RM), and technical drills at submaximal speed.

## LOAD_MANAGEMENT: Athletics Throwing Event Load Monitoring

phv_stages: [CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [athletics]
evidence_grade: B+

Throwing events (shot put, discus, javelin, hammer) place enormous rotational and impact forces on the musculoskeletal system. Javelin throw peak shoulder forces exceed 600N, and shot put peak forces on the wrist/elbow can reach 4,000N (Bartonietz 2000). Load monitoring must track both throw count and intensity. Weekly throw count limits by age: U15: 60–80 full-effort throws across all sessions, U17: 80–120, U19: 100–150. Technical/drill throws at submaximal effort (<80%) can be additional but should not exceed 2x the full-effort count. Session structure: never schedule max-effort throwing on consecutive days. A typical week might be: Day 1 — full throws (competition intensity), Day 2 — strength training (lower body), Day 3 — technical drills at 70–80%, Day 4 — strength (upper body + core), Day 5 — full throws with competition simulation, Days 6–7 — active recovery. Injury hotspots by event: javelin — shoulder (rotator cuff, labrum) and elbow (UCL); shot put — wrist, shoulder, knee (front leg); discus — low back, shoulder; hammer — low back, fingers. Prehab essentials: (1) Rotator cuff strengthening (daily, 3×15 internal/external rotation), (2) Core anti-rotation (Pallof press, 3×10 each side), (3) Hip mobility (90/90 rotations, pigeon stretches), (4) Eccentric wrist work for javelin and shot (3×12 wrist curls). Monitor throwing velocity — if competition-effort velocity drops >5% without explanation, suspect accumulated fatigue and reduce volume by 30% for one week. During growth spurts (PHV), reduce full-effort throws by 40% and emphasize technical development with lighter implements.

## PHV: Pre-PHV Training Priorities — Fundamental Movement Skills
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [PRE]
age_groups: [U13]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Pre-PHV athletes (typically ages 9–12, boys; 8–11, girls) should prioritize fundamental movement skill (FMS) development over sport-specific specialization. Research consistently shows that FMS competency established before PHV predicts athletic success and injury resilience in adolescence (Myer 2011, Lloyd & Oliver 2012). Training priorities: (1) Movement literacy — master the 7 fundamental movement patterns: squat, hinge, lunge, push, pull, rotate, locomote. Assess using the FMS screen or similar; target score >14/21 before introducing structured strength training. (2) Multi-directional speed — agility ladders, cone drills, tag games, multi-directional chasing games. Focus on deceleration and change-of-direction technique rather than maximal speed. (3) Bodyweight strength — push-ups, pull-ups, inverted rows, single-leg squats, bear crawls, crab walks. Volume: 2–3 sessions/week, 20–30 minutes per session. No external load above bodyweight until FMS competency demonstrated. (4) Coordination and balance — single-leg stance (target 30s eyes closed), beam walking, catching and throwing under movement, juggling, skipping variations. (5) Plyometric introduction — low-intensity only: hop scotch, skipping, jumping rope (up to 60 ground contacts/session), broad jumps for distance, single-leg hops. No depth jumps or drop jumps until post-PHV. (6) Flexibility — dynamic stretching pre-activity, light static stretching post-activity (hold 15–20s, no forcing). (7) Multi-sport participation — pre-PHV athletes should participate in 2–3 sports to develop diverse movement patterns. Early single-sport specialization before age 12 is associated with higher injury rates (2x risk) and earlier burnout (Jayanthi 2013). Training-to-competition ratio should be at least 2:1 (twice as much practice as competition).

## PHV: Mid-PHV Bone Stress and Growth Plate Protection
rec_types: [READINESS, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [CIRCA]
age_groups: [U13, U15]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

During PHV (Peak Height Velocity), rapid bone growth creates vulnerability at the growth plates (physes) and apophyses (tendon attachment points). Growth rates of 8–12 cm/year create a temporary period where bones grow faster than muscles and tendons can adapt, creating increased tension at attachment points (Mirwald 2002). Key vulnerabilities: (1) Osgood-Schlatter disease — tibial tuberosity (knee), prevalence 10–20% of athletic youth during PHV. Exacerbated by jumping, kicking, sprinting. Management: reduce jumping volume by 50%, avoid deep squats beyond 90°, ice post-activity. (2) Sever's disease — calcaneal apophysis (heel), common in running and jumping sports. Use heel cups, reduce running volume by 30–40%, switch to cushioned footwear. (3) Little Leaguer's shoulder/elbow — proximal humeral and medial epicondyle growth plates, vulnerable to throwing and overhead activities. (4) Stress fractures — tibia, metatarsals, and pars interarticularis are highest risk sites during PHV. Training modifications during PHV: reduce total training volume by 20–30% from pre-PHV levels. Eliminate or significantly reduce: depth jumps, heavy loaded squats, high-volume sprinting (>200m maximal per session), repetitive overhead throwing (>50 throws per session). Monitor growth velocity monthly using seated and standing height measurements. When growth rate exceeds 0.4 cm/month, implement precautionary loading reductions. Maintain strength training but shift to higher reps/lower load (3×12–15 at 50–65% 1RM). Prioritize flexibility — growth spurts reduce hamstring, quadriceps, and calf flexibility; stretch these groups daily (2×30s holds). Any persistent bony tenderness lasting >2 weeks warrants medical imaging to rule out growth plate injury.

## PHV: Mid-PHV Training Volume Reduction Guidelines
rec_types: [READINESS, DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [CIRCA]
age_groups: [U13, U15]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Specific volume reduction guidelines during the circa-PHV period (typically 12–14 for boys, 11–13 for girls) are critical for injury prevention while maintaining development. Total weekly training hours should be capped at: age in years minus 2 hours as a maximum (e.g., a 13-year-old: max 11 hours/week across all activities, Brenner 2007). Specific reductions by activity type: (1) High-intensity running — reduce weekly high-speed running volume by 30–40% compared to pre-PHV levels. Replace with lower-intensity technical work. (2) Jumping and plyometrics — cap ground contacts at 40–60 per session (vs 80–120 post-PHV). Eliminate depth jumps and reactive plyometrics entirely. Focus on landing mechanics — proper knee alignment, soft landings. (3) Strength training — maintain 2 sessions/week but reduce intensity to 50–65% 1RM with higher reps (12–15). Avoid heavy spinal loading (back squats, deadlifts at high percentage). Use goblet squats, trap bar deadlifts, and bodyweight progressions instead. (4) Sport-specific repetitive movements — reduce overhead throws by 40%, reduce kicking volume by 30%, reduce serve count by 30%. (5) Competition frequency — reduce to maximum 1 competition/week during peak growth velocity. Implement mandatory rest days: minimum 2 per week, no exceptions. Weekly periodization: never schedule 3 or more consecutive high-load days. Monitor subjective fatigue and pain daily using a 1–10 scale — if pain at any joint exceeds 4/10, modify or remove the aggravating activity. Growth-related pain is NOT normal training pain — it requires specific management, not "pushing through." Sleep needs increase during PHV to 9–11 hours; inadequate sleep compounds growth plate vulnerability.

## PHV: Post-PHV Return to Loading Protocols
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT, READINESS]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Post-PHV athletes (typically 1–2 years after peak growth velocity) can gradually return to full loading, but the transition must be progressive. Growth plate closure is not instantaneous — it occurs over 12–18 months after PHV, and during this period athletes remain at elevated risk compared to fully mature adults. Return-to-loading protocol: Phase 1 (months 1–3 post-PHV peak): increase training volume by 10% per week from PHV-reduced levels. Reintroduce depth jumps at low height (20–30cm boxes, max 40 contacts/session). Strength training progresses to 70–80% 1RM, 3×6–8 reps. Continue monitoring growth plate tenderness at knee (tibial tuberosity), heel (calcaneus), and shoulder. Phase 2 (months 4–6): training volume can approach pre-PHV levels. Plyometric volume increases to 60–80 ground contacts/session, including reactive plyometrics. Strength training progresses to 80–90% 1RM for major compound lifts. Introduce Olympic lift derivatives (hang clean, push press) with technique focus. Phase 3 (months 7–12): full loading permissible if no pain and all growth plate regions are symptom-free. Plyometric volume: 80–120 ground contacts/session. Max strength and power training fully introduced. Competition frequency can return to normal. Throughout all phases: maintain flexibility work (growth-related tightness can persist for 6–12 months post-PHV). Monitor bilateral strength asymmetries — growth can create temporary imbalances; address any >15% difference. Reassess maturity offset every 6 months using Mirwald equation until 2 years post-PHV. Key benchmarks for clearance: pain-free single-leg hop (LSI >90%), bilateral CMJ within 5% of age-matched norms, full flexibility in hamstrings (SLR >80°) and quadriceps (prone heel-to-buttock <10cm gap).

## PHV: Monitoring — When to Re-Assess Maturity Offset
rec_types: [READINESS]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Maturity offset estimation (years from PHV) should be recalculated regularly to guide training prescription. The Mirwald equation (2002) uses standing height, sitting height, leg length, body mass, and chronological age to estimate years from PHV. Re-assessment schedule: every 3 months during ages 10–15 for boys, 9–14 for girls. More frequent assessment (monthly) is warranted when: (1) growth rate exceeds 0.4 cm/month (indicating approaching or at PHV), (2) athlete reports new onset of growth-related pain (Osgood-Schlatter, Sever's), (3) observed coordination changes ("adolescent awkwardness" — temporary loss of previously mastered skills). Measurement protocol: take standing and sitting height at the same time of day (preferably morning — spinal compression can reduce height by 1–2cm throughout the day). Average 3 measurements for each. Use a stadiometer or standardized wall measurement with head in Frankfurt plane. Record body mass in light clothing, same scale each time. Key decision points based on maturity offset: Pre-PHV (offset < -1.0 years) — focus on FMS, multi-sport, bodyweight strength. Circa-PHV (offset -1.0 to +1.0) — reduce volume, protect growth plates, maintain but don't intensify. Post-PHV (offset > +1.0) — gradual return to full loading. Important limitations: Mirwald equation has a standard error of ±0.5 years, so athletes near transitions should be treated conservatively. For early and late maturers, bio-banding (grouping by maturity status rather than chronological age) should be considered for competition to reduce injury risk and improve development (Cumming 2017). Athletes who are early maturers may appear ready for advanced training but their tendons and ligaments may still be developing — maintain precautions until 18 months post-PHV.

## PHV: Psychological Development Considerations
rec_types: [WELLBEING]
phv_stages: [CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

PHV coincides with significant psychological and psychosocial changes that directly affect training and performance. During circa-PHV, athletes may experience: (1) Reduced self-efficacy — previously mastered skills may temporarily deteriorate due to changing body proportions, leading to frustration and decreased confidence. Coaches should normalize this experience: "Your body is upgrading — temporary bugs are expected." (2) Increased social comparison — adolescents become hyper-aware of physical differences between early and late maturers. Early maturers may dominate due to size advantages, while late maturers (who often become more skilled long-term) may lose motivation. Use bio-banding competition and emphasize process over outcome. (3) Identity formation — athletes begin defining themselves through sport. Excessive pressure or early specialization can create fragile athletic identity. Encourage multi-dimensional identity: athlete + student + friend + other interests. (4) Emotional regulation — prefrontal cortex development lags behind limbic system maturation, creating emotional volatility. Expect increased frustration, anger during competition, and difficulty accepting feedback. Teach emotional regulation techniques: box breathing (4-4-4-4 pattern), reframing self-talk, pre-performance routines. (5) Autonomy needs — adolescents need increasing control over their training. Involve them in goal setting, session planning, and recovery decisions. Athlete-led warm-ups, choice in drill selection, and self-monitoring (RPE, wellness diaries) build intrinsic motivation. (6) Sleep disruption — circadian rhythm shifts during puberty (melatonin release delays 1–2 hours), making early morning training counterproductive. Where possible, schedule high-quality sessions after 10am for circa-PHV athletes. Research indicates that athletes who feel psychologically supported through PHV show 30% better retention rates in sport (Wylleman 2011).

## AGE_BAND: U13 Training Volume and Intensity Limits
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT, READINESS]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA]
age_groups: [U13]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

U13 athletes (ages 11–13) are typically pre-PHV or early circa-PHV and require carefully controlled training parameters. Weekly total training volume should not exceed the "age minus 2" rule: maximum 11 hours/week across all organized sport and training activities (Brenner 2016). Session duration: 60–75 minutes for technical/tactical sessions, 30–45 minutes for physical development sessions. Intensity distribution: 70–80% of total training time should be at low-to-moderate intensity (RPE 3–5/10), 15–20% at moderate-to-high (RPE 6–7), and maximum 5–10% at high intensity (RPE 8–10). Specific limits: (1) Maximal sprints (>95% effort): maximum 150–200m total volume per session, 2 sessions/week maximum. (2) Plyometrics: 30–50 ground contacts per session, low-intensity only (skipping, hopping, bounding — no depth jumps). 2 sessions/week. (3) Strength training: bodyweight exercises primarily. If external load is used, maximum 50–60% of estimated 1RM, 2×12–15 reps. No 1RM testing. 2 sessions/week. (4) Sport-specific repetitive movements: limit to volumes that do not cause pain — overhead throws <40 per session, serves <30 per session, kicks <50 per session. Competition: maximum 1 competitive event per week. Training-to-competition ratio should be at least 3:1 (three hours of practice per hour of competition). Rest days: minimum 2 full rest days per week, with at least 1 day completely free of organized physical activity. Annual structure: at least 2–3 months per year free from primary sport to allow physical and psychological recovery (DiFiori 2014). Sleep: 9–11 hours per night target. Monitor happiness and enjoyment — at this age, fun and social connection are the primary retention factors.

## AGE_BAND: U13 Multi-Sport Participation Benefits
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA]
age_groups: [U13]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Multi-sport participation before age 13 is one of the strongest predictors of long-term athletic success and injury prevention. Research from German Olympic athletes found that 85% participated in multiple sports during childhood, with specialization occurring on average at age 14 (Güllich 2014). Benefits of multi-sport participation: (1) Diverse movement patterns — each sport develops unique motor skills. Football develops bilateral foot coordination, basketball develops vertical power and spatial awareness, tennis develops unilateral rotational power, athletics develops linear speed and jumping, padel develops hand-eye coordination and reaction time. Cross-transfer of these skills creates a broader movement vocabulary. (2) Reduced overuse injury — single-sport athletes at U13 have 1.5–2.5x higher overuse injury rates compared to multi-sport peers (Jayanthi 2013). Different sports load different tissues, preventing repetitive strain on the same structures. (3) Burnout prevention — early specializers show significantly higher burnout rates by age 15–16. Multi-sport athletes report higher enjoyment, lower perceived pressure, and greater intrinsic motivation. (4) Better long-term performance — multiple studies show that athletes who specialized later (age 14–16) outperform early specializers by age 18–20 in most sports (Bridge & Toms 2013). Exceptions where earlier specialization may be beneficial: gymnastics, figure skating, diving (sports with peak performance before full maturity). Recommended approach for U13: participate in 2–3 organized sports per season, with a primary sport receiving 50–60% of training time and secondary sports filling the remainder. Avoid year-round participation in any single sport. Seasonal sport rotation example: football (fall/winter), athletics (spring), tennis/padel (summer). If an athlete has a clear preference for one sport, maintain at least 1 complementary sport year-round and include 2–3 months of rest from the primary sport annually.

## AGE_BAND: U15 Strength Introduction Protocols
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Age 13–15 is the critical window for introducing structured resistance training, which is both safe and highly beneficial when properly supervised (Lloyd & Faigenbaum 2016). Strength training in youth reduces injury risk by 50–66% and improves motor performance across all sports. Introduction protocol by phase: Phase 1 — Movement Competency (4–6 weeks): Focus entirely on bodyweight exercises and movement patterns. Master: goblet squat (assess for knee valgus, depth), hip hinge (Romanian deadlift pattern with dowel), push-up (full range, controlled), inverted row, lunge (forward, lateral, reverse), single-leg balance. Assessment: athlete must demonstrate competent form for 10+ consecutive reps before adding external load. Phase 2 — Load Introduction (6–8 weeks): Begin with 40–50% estimated 1RM, 2×10–12 reps. Exercises: trap bar deadlift (safer than conventional for learning), goblet squat with dumbbell/kettlebell, dumbbell bench press, seated row, dumbbell step-up. Progress load by maximum 5% per week when form is maintained. 2 sessions/week, separated by 48+ hours. Phase 3 — Progressive Loading (ongoing): Progress to 60–75% 1RM, 3×8–10 reps. Introduce barbell exercises: back squat (with rack, safety bars, and competent spotter), bench press, barbell row. Periodize: 4-week mesocycles with progressive overload followed by 1 deload week (reduce volume by 30%). Phase 4 — Power Integration: Once base strength is established (after 3–6 months of Phase 3), introduce power exercises: jump squats (20–30% 1RM), medicine ball throws, Olympic lift derivatives (hang clean, push press) with PVC pipe or empty bar first. Never sacrifice form for load. Red flags to stop and regress: pain during exercise, inability to complete prescribed reps with good form, compensation patterns (excessive lean, knee valgus, asymmetric movement).

## AGE_BAND: U19 Senior Transition Physical Preparation

phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

The U19 (17–19 years) to senior transition is the most physically demanding period in an athlete's development, with injury rates peaking during the transition year (Bult 2018). Physical preparation must address the increased demands of senior competition: faster game speed, stronger opponents, and higher training loads. Key areas: (1) Strength benchmarks — athletes transitioning to senior level should aim for: back squat 1.5–2.0x body mass, trap bar deadlift 2.0–2.5x body mass, bench press 1.0–1.3x body mass, pull-ups 8–12+ reps. These benchmarks correlate with reduced injury risk in senior competition (Suchomel 2016). (2) Speed development — linear speed peaks naturally around 18–21 years. Focus on acceleration mechanics (0–10m, target <1.8s) and maximum velocity development (30m flying, target <3.4s for team sport athletes). Include resisted sprint training (sled pulls at 10–20% body mass) and assisted sprinting (downhill, band-assisted). (3) Repeated sprint ability — senior sport demands greater repeated sprint capacity. Train with protocols matching sport demands: e.g., 6×30m with 25s recovery for football, 10×20m with 20s recovery for basketball. (4) Robustness training — prepare for the physical contact and collision demands of senior sport. Include: loaded carries (farmer walks, sled pushes), contact conditioning (if applicable), neck strengthening (3×/week for contact sports), trunk stability under fatigue. (5) Training load tolerance — gradually increase weekly training hours over 12–18 months to match senior squad volumes. If senior squad trains 15 hours/week but U19 trains 10 hours/week, bridge this gap over 3–4 months, not overnight. Monitor ACWR meticulously during transition — the most dangerous period is weeks 4–8 after joining senior training. (6) Position-specific preparation — work with senior coaches to understand position-specific physical demands and train specifically for them 6–12 months before transition.

## RECOVERY: Cold Water Immersion Protocols
rec_types: [RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Cold water immersion (CWI) is one of the most studied recovery modalities in sport science. Evidence summary: CWI is effective for reducing muscle soreness by 15–20% and perceived fatigue following high-intensity exercise (Leeder 2012). Optimal protocol: water temperature 10–15°C, immersion time 10–15 minutes, submerge to waist or chest level. Colder is NOT better — temperatures below 10°C increase discomfort without additional recovery benefit and may increase injury risk (cold shock). When to use CWI: (1) After matches or high-intensity competition — particularly effective when there is a short turnaround (<48 hours) to the next competition. Reduces next-day soreness and improves subsequent performance by 2–3%. (2) During tournament play with multiple same-day matches — 10–12 min at 12–15°C between matches (if >4 hours between). (3) After particularly demanding training sessions (high eccentric load, extensive high-speed running). When NOT to use CWI: (1) After strength training aimed at hypertrophy — CWI blunts the inflammatory response that drives muscle growth. Chronic use after strength training reduces strength gains by 10–15% over 12 weeks (Roberts 2015). (2) During pre-season when the goal is adaptation — some inflammation is necessary for training adaptation. Limit CWI to 1–2 times/week during preparatory phases. (3) Immediately before training or competition — cold exposure reduces power output by 3–5% for up to 45 minutes. Youth-specific considerations: U15 and younger should use shorter durations (8–10 minutes) and slightly warmer temperatures (13–15°C) due to higher surface-area-to-mass ratio causing faster core temperature drop. Always supervise youth CWI. Contrast water therapy (alternating 1 min cold 10–15°C / 2 min warm 38–40°C, repeated 3–4 times) is an alternative that some athletes prefer and shows comparable results to CWI alone.

## RECOVERY: Compression Garments for Youth Athletes
rec_types: [RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

Compression garments (tights, sleeves, socks) are widely used in sport for recovery, with moderate evidence supporting their effectiveness. Meta-analysis findings (Hill 2014): compression garments worn during recovery (not during exercise) reduce perceived muscle soreness by 10–15%, reduce swelling (circumference measures), and show small positive effects on subsequent performance (1–3% improvement in power/sprint tasks when measured 24–48 hours post-exercise). Mechanisms: improved venous return, reduced muscle oscillation, possible improvement in proprioception and muscle alignment, placebo/psychological confidence effect. Optimal usage protocol: (1) Post-exercise — don compression garments within 30 minutes of finishing exercise. Wear for 2–12 hours post-exercise for best effect. Overnight wear is acceptable if comfortable. (2) During travel — wearing compression during extended travel (flights >2 hours, bus trips) reduces leg swelling and perceived fatigue upon arrival. Particularly valuable for tournament travel. (3) Between matches — wear during rest periods at tournaments. Correct fit is critical — garments should feel snug but not restrictive. Pressure should be graduated (highest at ankles, decreasing toward thigh). Incorrectly sized garments provide no benefit and may restrict blood flow. Youth-specific considerations: (1) Growth — purchase garments that fit current size, not "to grow into." Compression that is too loose provides no benefit. Replace when growth makes them too tight (usually every 6–12 months during PHV). (2) Cost-effectiveness — for youth budgets, compression socks provide the best value as lower limbs benefit most. Full tights are secondary priority. (3) Not a substitute — compression should complement, not replace, active recovery, sleep, and nutrition. Evidence grade is B because while effects are consistent, they are small and there is significant placebo contribution.

## RECOVERY: Hydration Protocols for Training and Competition
rec_types: [RECOVERY, READINESS]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Hydration status directly impacts performance: as little as 2% body mass loss from dehydration reduces endurance performance by 4–6%, impairs decision-making, and increases perceived exertion (Sawka 2007). Youth athletes are at higher risk because: higher surface-area-to-mass ratio increases sweating relative to body size, lower sweat rate efficiency, reduced thirst perception compared to adults. Pre-training hydration: consume 5–7 ml/kg body mass 2–4 hours before training (e.g., 60kg athlete: 300–420ml). Urine should be pale yellow (color chart 1–3) before starting. During training: drink 150–250ml every 15–20 minutes during exercise. For sessions <60 minutes in temperate conditions: water is sufficient. For sessions >60 minutes or in heat (>25°C): add electrolytes — 500–700mg sodium per liter. Sport drinks (6–8% carbohydrate solution) are beneficial for sessions >75 minutes at moderate-to-high intensity. Post-training: aim to replace 150% of fluid lost within 2–4 hours. Weigh before and after training to estimate fluid loss — 1kg loss = 1 liter deficit, so drink 1.5 liters. Include sodium (500–700mg/L) to improve fluid retention. Heat-specific protocols: acclimatize over 10–14 days of progressive heat exposure. In heat >30°C, increase fluid intake by 50%. Use cold beverages and ice slurries before and during exercise — cold fluid reduces core temperature and improves endurance in heat by 2–4% (Siegel 2010). Pre-cool with cold towels on neck and forearms. Warning signs of dehydration: thirst (already indicates 1–2% dehydration), dark urine, headache, dizziness, unusually high heart rate for given intensity. Team-level monitoring: spot-check urine specific gravity (USG) before training — target <1.020. USG >1.025 = significantly dehydrated, modify session intensity.

## RECOVERY: Sleep Hygiene for Youth Athletes
rec_types: [RECOVERY, WELLBEING]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Sleep is the single most powerful recovery tool available, yet youth athletes consistently undersleep. Sleep recommendations by age: U13: 9–11 hours, U15: 8.5–10 hours, U17: 8–10 hours, U19+: 7.5–9 hours (Hirshkowitz 2015). Performance impact of insufficient sleep: reaction time decreases 10–15% after one night of <6 hours sleep, sprint speed decreases 3–4%, accuracy in sport-specific skills decreases 5–10%, injury risk increases 1.7x with <8 hours sleep in adolescent athletes (Milewski 2014). Technology optimization: (1) Blue light — screens emit light at 450–490nm wavelength that suppresses melatonin production by up to 50%. Implement strict "no screens" policy 60–90 minutes before target sleep time. If screens must be used, blue-light blocking glasses or device night mode reduce impact by approximately 50%. (2) Social media — set app time limits; the urge to check notifications is a primary cause of delayed sleep onset in 13–19 year olds. Charge phones outside the bedroom. Environment optimization: (1) Temperature — optimal sleep temperature is 18–20°C. Cool rooms improve deep sleep duration by 15–20%. (2) Darkness — complete darkness is ideal. Use blackout curtains; even dim light (nightlight, standby LEDs) can suppress melatonin by 10–15%. (3) Noise — white noise machines or earplugs for noisy environments. (4) Consistency — same wake time every day (including weekends), within a 30-minute window. The circadian clock is set by wake time more than sleep time. Sleep routine protocol: fixed routine 30–60 minutes before bed — shower/bath (raises then drops core temperature, promoting sleepiness), light reading, breathing exercises (4-7-8 technique), gratitude journaling. Napping guidelines: 20–30 minute naps between 1–3pm are beneficial (power nap). Avoid naps >45 minutes or after 4pm — these disrupt nighttime sleep. Post-competition sleep: adrenaline from evening competition delays sleep onset by 1–2 hours. Use cool-down routine, magnesium supplement (200–400mg glycinate), tart cherry juice (contains natural melatonin), and relaxation techniques.

## RECOVERY: Nutrition Timing — Pre, During, and Post Training Windows
rec_types: [RECOVERY, READINESS]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Nutrient timing directly impacts training quality, recovery speed, and adaptation. Youth athletes have higher relative energy needs than adults due to growth demands. Pre-training window (2–4 hours before): consume a meal containing 1–2g/kg carbohydrate, 0.3g/kg protein, low fat and fiber (to avoid GI distress). Examples for 60kg athlete: 2 slices toast with banana and honey + glass of milk (~90g carbs, 18g protein). If <60 minutes before training: small snack of 0.5–1g/kg carbs — banana, energy bar, or sports drink. Avoid high-fat, high-fiber foods within 2 hours of training. During training: sessions <60 minutes — water only sufficient. Sessions 60–90 minutes moderate intensity — optional: 30g carbs/hour from sports drink. Sessions >90 minutes or high intensity — 30–60g carbs/hour from sports drink, gels, or easily digestible food (jelly babies, dried fruit). Post-training window (0–2 hours — the "recovery window"): the most critical timing window. Targets: 1.0–1.2g/kg carbohydrate + 0.3–0.4g/kg protein within 30 minutes, then a full meal within 2 hours. This timing maximizes glycogen resynthesis (2x faster than delayed feeding) and muscle protein synthesis (Ivy 2002). Examples for 60kg athlete post-training: immediately — 500ml chocolate milk (40g carbs, 20g protein), then full meal within 2 hours — pasta with chicken and vegetables. For youth athletes specifically: never skip post-training nutrition — growing bodies need recovery fuel AND growth fuel simultaneously. Total daily protein: 1.2–1.6g/kg/day distributed across 4–5 meals/snacks (0.25–0.4g/kg per meal). Total daily carbohydrate: moderate training days 5–7g/kg, heavy training days 7–10g/kg, competition days 8–12g/kg. Emphasize whole foods over supplements — supplements are rarely necessary for youth athletes and are largely unregulated.

## RECOVERY: Foam Rolling and Self-Myofascial Release
rec_types: [RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Foam rolling (self-myofascial release) has become ubiquitous in sport, with growing evidence supporting specific applications. What the evidence shows (Wiewelhove 2019): foam rolling for 60–120 seconds per muscle group reduces DOMS (delayed onset muscle soreness) by 10–15%, improves range of motion by 4–7% without decreasing performance (unlike static stretching which can reduce power by 3–5%), and may slightly improve sprint and power performance when used as part of warm-up. Optimal protocol — pre-training: 30–60 seconds per muscle group, moderate pressure, at a rate of approximately 1 inch per second. Target: quadriceps, hamstrings, calves, IT band, glutes, thoracic spine. This can replace static stretching in warm-ups to gain ROM benefits without power decrements. Post-training recovery: 60–120 seconds per muscle group, moderate-to-firm pressure. Focus on muscles most loaded during the session. Can be combined with static stretching — foam roll first, then stretch for 30 seconds; this combination produces greater ROM gains than either alone. Pain threshold: target a "discomfort" level of 6–7/10 — enough to feel pressure and mild discomfort but not sharp pain. If sharp pain occurs, reduce pressure or avoid that area (may indicate injury requiring clinical assessment). Tools: standard density foam rollers for beginners, higher-density rollers for experienced users, lacrosse balls or massage balls for targeted work (plantar fascia, glutes, upper back). Vibrating rollers show slightly greater benefit than standard rollers (approximately 5% greater ROM improvement). Youth-specific notes: introduce foam rolling technique with supervision — improper use can cause bruising or exacerbate injuries. Avoid rolling directly over bony prominences or growth plate areas during PHV. Foam rolling is NOT a treatment for growth-related pain (Osgood-Schlatter, Sever's) — seek medical guidance for these conditions. Frequency: daily foam rolling is safe and beneficial; 5–10 minutes is sufficient.

## RECOVERY: Active Recovery vs Passive Rest — Decision Framework
rec_types: [RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Choosing between active recovery and passive (complete) rest depends on the type of fatigue, training phase, and individual athlete status. Decision framework: Use ACTIVE recovery when: (1) Muscle soreness is moderate (3–5/10 on VAS) without joint pain — light movement increases blood flow and accelerates metabolite clearance. (2) Between matches or competitions with 24–48 hour turnaround — 15–25 minutes of low-intensity cycling, swimming, or walking at 50–60% HRmax. (3) During in-season when training frequency is high — active recovery maintains movement quality and prevents stiffness. (4) For psychological benefit — some athletes feel better with gentle movement than complete inactivity. Active recovery protocols: cycling 15–20 min at <60% HRmax, pool walking/swimming 15–20 min at low intensity, yoga/mobility flow 20–30 min, light sport-specific technical work (<50% intensity for 20–30 min). Use PASSIVE rest when: (1) Injury suspected — any acute pain, swelling, or limited range of motion. Movement could worsen the condition. (2) Illness — fever, respiratory infection, gastrointestinal illness. Complete rest + medical evaluation. (3) Severe muscle soreness (>7/10) — indicates potential muscle damage beyond normal training adaptation; active recovery may prolong recovery. (4) Accumulated fatigue across multiple weeks — when wellness scores are consistently low (<12/20 for 5+ consecutive days), a full rest day (or multiple days) is more beneficial than active recovery. (5) Mental/emotional exhaustion — sometimes the best recovery is doing nothing sport-related. (6) Sleep deprivation (<6 hours previous night) — prioritize napping over light exercise. Hybrid approach: for most training weeks, schedule 1 active recovery day and 1 complete rest day. During intensified training blocks, increase to 2 rest days. During deload weeks, every non-training day should be active recovery. Listen to the athlete — if they consistently feel worse after active recovery sessions, switch to passive rest.

## RECOVERY: Psychological Recovery — Managing Mental Fatigue
rec_types: [RECOVERY, WELLBEING]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

Mental fatigue is increasingly recognized as a performance limiter independent of physical fatigue. Research shows that 30 minutes of cognitively demanding activity before exercise reduces endurance performance by 5% and impairs decision-making by 10–15% (Marcora 2009). For student-athletes, this is critical: training immediately after exams, difficult classes, or prolonged study produces lower-quality training. Sources of mental fatigue in youth athletes: (1) Academic demands — exams, homework, classroom concentration. (2) Social media — constant connectivity creates low-level cognitive drain. (3) Travel — commuting to/from training, tournament travel. (4) Decision fatigue — juggling schedules, social obligations, sport commitments. (5) Emotional stress — family dynamics, peer relationships, selection anxiety. Mental recovery strategies: (1) Micro-breaks — 5-minute breaks every 25–30 minutes during study (Pomodoro technique). Stand, move, look at something far away (reduces screen eye strain). (2) Nature exposure — 20 minutes in nature reduces cortisol by 12–15% and improves mood (Hunter 2019). Post-training walk in a park, training outdoors when possible. (3) Mindfulness — 10 minutes daily mindfulness meditation improves attention, reduces anxiety, and enhances recovery perception. Apps like Headspace or Calm provide guided sessions appropriate for youth. Start with 3–5 minutes and build up. (4) Social connection — unstructured social time (not via screens) with friends or family is restorative. Schedule this like training. (5) Creative outlets — music, art, reading fiction, gaming (time-limited) — activities that are absorbing but not cognitively demanding. (6) Digital detox — 1–2 hours daily completely screen-free. One full "digital detox" day per month. Implementation: coaches should ask about mental state, not just physical readiness. If mental fatigue score is >7/10, reduce training complexity (fewer tactical decisions, more physical or technical work) rather than canceling the session entirely.

## ACADEMIC: Cognitive Load and Training Quality Interaction
rec_types: [READINESS, WELLBEING]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Cognitive load from academic work directly impacts subsequent training quality. Studies show that 90+ minutes of concentrated academic work increases perceived exertion during training by 12–15% at the same physical intensity, reduces decision-making accuracy by 8–12%, and impairs reaction time by 5–8% (Smith 2016). This has practical implications for scheduling and session design. Scheduling guidelines: (1) Optimal training time after school — allow at least 30–45 minutes of mental decompression between end of school and training start. This buffer allows cognitive resources to partially recover. (2) Post-exam training — on exam days, reduce tactical complexity of training sessions by 30%. Focus on physical conditioning or technical repetition rather than game-intelligence demands. (3) Morning vs afternoon — if dual sessions are required, place the most cognitively demanding training (tactical, decision-making) in the morning before school, and physical/conditioning sessions after school. (4) Study-training sequencing — avoid scheduling heavy study sessions immediately before important training or matches. Create a "bridge activity" between study and sport: 10 minutes of light physical activity (walking, dynamic stretching) to shift cognitive gears. Session design modifications based on academic load: When cognitive load is HIGH (exam week, project deadlines): focus on closed skills (drills with predictable patterns), physical conditioning, strength training, and individual technical work. Reduce small-sided games, tactical sessions, and anything requiring high decision-making load. When cognitive load is LOW (holidays, light school weeks): maximize open skill development, tactical games, match simulation, and competitive scenarios that build game intelligence. Monitoring: ask athletes to rate their "brain tiredness" on a 1–10 scale alongside physical readiness. If cognitive fatigue >7/10, automatically reduce session complexity. Track the relationship between academic calendar and training quality — many coaches find performance dips correlate with exam periods, allowing proactive planning.

## ACADEMIC: Exam Week Training Modifications
rec_types: [READINESS, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Exam periods represent the highest dual-stress period for student-athletes, combining intense cognitive load with disrupted sleep, increased anxiety, and often poor nutrition. A structured exam-week training protocol prevents detraining while supporting academic performance. Research shows that moderate exercise during exam periods actually improves cognitive function, memory consolidation, and stress management compared to complete rest (Hillman 2008). Modified training protocol during exam weeks: Volume reduction — decrease total training time by 40–50% from normal weekly volume. Maintain frequency (number of sessions) but reduce duration: 60-minute sessions become 30–40 minutes. Intensity — maintain moderate intensity (RPE 5–7/10) in remaining sessions. Avoid maximal efforts that create excessive physiological stress on top of psychological stress. Session type — prioritize: (1) Aerobic exercise at moderate intensity (20–30 min jog, bike, swim) — best for stress reduction and cognitive enhancement. (2) Light strength maintenance (2×6–8 reps at 70–75% 1RM, major compound lifts only). (3) Enjoyable, low-pressure technical work (shooting practice, hitting drills with music). Avoid: high-intensity interval training (additional cortisol spike), competitive situations (additional stress), complex tactical sessions (cognitive overload). Timing — schedule training as a study break, ideally mid-afternoon: 10am–2pm study block → 2pm–3pm training → 3pm–7pm study block. Exercise before an evening study session improves retention compared to exercise after studying. Duration of modifications — begin 2–3 days before the first exam and maintain throughout the exam period. Resume normal training 48 hours after the final exam, but rebuild volume over 1 week (not immediately back to full load). Nutrition during exams: maintain normal training nutrition even if training less — the brain consumes 20% of daily calories; reducing food intake during exams impairs cognitive performance.

## ACADEMIC: Study-Training Daily Scheduling
rec_types: [WELLBEING, READINESS]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

Effective daily scheduling for student-athletes requires intentional time-blocking that respects both cognitive and physical recovery needs. Research on dual-career athletes shows that structured scheduling reduces perceived stress by 25–30% and improves both academic and sport outcomes compared to unstructured approaches (Stambulova 2015). Template daily schedule for school-day training: 6:30–7:00 — wake, morning routine (no phone first 20 min). 7:00–7:30 — breakfast (high-quality carbs + protein). 7:30–8:00 — commute (use for light review or mental preparation, not screen time). 8:00–15:00 — school (prioritize focus, complete as much work during school hours as possible). 15:00–15:30 — transition buffer: snack (carbs + protein), change, mental decompression (no study). 15:30–17:00 — training session. 17:00–17:30 — post-training nutrition, shower. 17:30–18:00 — commute/transition. 18:00–18:30 — dinner. 18:30–20:30 — study block 1 (90 min focused study using Pomodoro: 25 min work / 5 min break). 20:30–21:00 — free time (social, non-screen leisure). 21:00–21:30 — study block 2 (30 min review or light reading). 21:30–22:00 — sleep routine (no screens). 22:00 — lights out (targeting 8.5 hours sleep for 15–17 year olds). Key principles: (1) Protect the transition buffer — jumping from school to training without decompression increases injury risk and reduces training quality. (2) Front-load study intensity — the first study block should contain the hardest material; use the evening mini-block for review only. (3) Weekend loading — shift heavier training to weekend mornings (when there is no prior cognitive load), and heavier study to weekend afternoons. (4) Non-negotiable sleep — no study or training schedule should compromise sleep below 8 hours for youth athletes. If unable to complete schoolwork within these blocks, discuss workload reduction with teachers rather than cutting sleep.

## ACADEMIC: Academic Stress Biomarkers and Readiness Impact
rec_types: [READINESS, WELLBEING]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

Academic stress produces measurable physiological responses that directly impact athletic readiness and injury risk. Understanding these biomarkers allows coaches and athletes to make informed training modifications. Key biomarkers affected by academic stress: (1) Cortisol — academic stress elevates salivary cortisol by 25–40% compared to non-exam periods (Weekes 2006). Elevated cortisol impairs muscle recovery (slowing protein synthesis by 15–20%), disrupts sleep architecture (reducing deep sleep), suppresses immune function (illness rates increase 2–3x during exams), and promotes catabolic state (potential muscle mass loss if prolonged). (2) Heart Rate Variability (HRV) — resting HRV decreases by 8–15% during high academic stress periods, indicating sympathetic nervous system dominance. This is the same HRV pattern seen with physical overtraining, meaning academic stress and training stress are CUMULATIVE, not independent. An athlete showing normal HRV during training but suppressed HRV during exams is effectively in an overtrained state even without increased training load. (3) Sleep quality — academic stress reduces sleep efficiency by 10–15%, increases sleep onset latency by 15–25 minutes, and reduces REM sleep (important for motor learning consolidation). (4) Subjective wellbeing — mood disturbance, increased anxiety, reduced motivation. These are leading indicators that precede physical symptoms by 2–5 days. Practical application for readiness systems: combine academic stress self-report (1–10 scale) with standard physical readiness metrics. If combined readiness score (physical + academic) drops below threshold, auto-modify training prescription: reduce volume by 20–30%, avoid maximal intensity, increase recovery components of sessions. Athletes with consistently high academic stress (boarding school, AP/IB programs) should have baseline training volumes set 10–15% lower than peers with lighter academic loads, with saved capacity available for exam period reductions. Educate athletes that academic stress is "real" stress — it's not in their head, and it genuinely affects their body and performance.

## ACADEMIC: Time Management Frameworks for Student-Athletes
rec_types: [WELLBEING]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

Effective time management is a learnable skill that predicts both academic success and athletic longevity in dual-career athletes (Aquilina 2013). Youth athletes who develop strong time management by age 15–16 show significantly better outcomes at the senior transition. Core frameworks: (1) The Eisenhower Matrix — categorize all tasks into 4 quadrants: Urgent+Important (do immediately: exam tomorrow, injury treatment), Important+Not Urgent (schedule: consistent training, long-term study, sleep), Urgent+Not Important (delegate or minimize: social obligations, admin tasks), Neither (eliminate or strictly limit: excessive social media, aimless browsing). Most student-athletes spend too much time in quadrant 4 and too little in quadrant 2. (2) Weekly planning ritual — every Sunday evening (15–20 minutes): review upcoming week's academic deadlines, training schedule, and competitions. Identify potential conflicts (exam + match day, multiple assignments due). Pre-plan study blocks and meal prep. This single habit reduces weekly stress by 20–25% because surprises become expected events. (3) The "Big 3" daily focus — each morning, identify the 3 most important tasks for the day (1 academic, 1 sport, 1 personal). Complete these before anything else. This prevents the feeling of "being busy but accomplishing nothing." (4) Time auditing — for one week, track actual time spent in 30-minute blocks. Most athletes are shocked to find 2–4 hours/day of unaccounted time (usually screen time). This awareness alone typically reclaims 1–2 productive hours. (5) "Protected time" — establish non-negotiable blocks: sleep (not compressed for any reason), post-training nutrition (even 15 minutes), 1 hour/day completely free from sport and study (mental health). Tools: digital calendar with color-coding (school = blue, sport = orange, free = green), physical planner for daily task lists. Implementation: teach these frameworks at the start of each season, revisit monthly. Pair younger athletes with older mentors who demonstrate successful dual-career management.

## ACADEMIC: School Holiday Training Camps
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT, READINESS]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

School holidays provide a unique opportunity for increased training volume without academic stress, but loading must be carefully managed to avoid injury from sudden volume spikes. Loading framework for holiday training camps: Pre-camp preparation (1 week before): do NOT increase training suddenly at the start of holidays. The week before camp should maintain normal in-season volume to establish baseline ACWR. Camp week 1: increase total volume by maximum 20–30% above normal weekly training hours. If normal week = 10 hours training, camp week 1 = maximum 12–13 hours. Distribute across 5–6 days with 1 full rest day. (3) Ignoring accumulation fatigue — monitor daily wellness scores; if team average drops below baseline for 2 consecutive days, implement a recovery day. (4) Neglecting sleep — camp social dynamics (shared rooms, excitement) often reduce sleep. Enforce quiet hours and monitor sleep duration. (5) Dehydration — holiday camps often occur in summer heat. Increase fluid monitoring and availability. Post-camp recovery: allow 3–5 days of reduced training before returning to normal schedule. Monitor for delayed-onset injuries in the 2 weeks post-camp — the adaptation from camp loading continues after camp ends.

## PERIODIZATION: Pre-Season Block 1 — Aerobic Base Building
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Pre-season Block 1 (typically weeks 1–3 of a 6-week pre-season) focuses on re-establishing aerobic fitness after the off-season. This phase sets the physiological foundation for all subsequent training. Aerobic fitness underpins recovery capacity, repeat sprint ability, and training load tolerance across all field and court sports (Buchheit & Laursen 2013). Training prescription: (1) Continuous methods — steady-state runs at 70–80% HRmax, 20–40 minutes. Start at 20 minutes and add 5 minutes per session. 2–3 sessions/week. Builds cardiac output and mitochondrial density. (2) Long intervals — work periods 3–5 minutes at 85–90% HRmax, with 2–3 minutes active recovery. Start with 3×3 min and progress to 4×5 min over the block. These develop VO2max more effectively than continuous running. (3) Game-based conditioning — small-sided games (SSG) at controlled intensity can simultaneously develop aerobic fitness and sport-specific skills. SSG prescription for aerobic development: larger pitch sizes, more players (6v6 or 7v7), continuous play for 4–6 minute periods, active recovery between sets. (4) Cross-training — cycling, swimming, rowing can supplement running-based conditioning. Useful for athletes with lower limb niggles from off-season detraining. Volume targets for Block 1: total weekly running distance should start at 50–60% of peak in-season volume and progress to 75–80% by end of block. Weekly sRPE (session RPE × duration): start at 60% of in-season average and build 10–15% per week. High-speed running (>5.5 m/s) should be limited to <30% of in-season average — this is an aerobic block, not a speed block. Strength training during this block: hypertrophy phase (3×10–12 at 65–75% 1RM), 3 sessions/week. Focus on rebuilding muscle cross-section lost during off-season. Include injury prevention work: Nordic hamstring curls, Copenhagen adductor exercises, single-leg balance progressions. Monitoring: Yo-Yo IR1 test at start and end of Block 1 — aim for 5–10% improvement.

## PERIODIZATION: Pre-Season Block 2 — Sport-Specific Preparation
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Pre-season Block 2 (typically weeks 3–5) transitions from general fitness to sport-specific conditioning, integrating tactical and technical work at increasing intensity. This block bridges the gap between aerobic base and competition readiness. Training prescription: (1) High-intensity interval training (HIIT) — replace long intervals with shorter, sport-specific intervals. Protocols: 10–15 second sprints with 45–50 second recovery (ratio 1:3–4), repeated 6–10 times. 30/30 protocol (30s at 90–95% HRmax / 30s active recovery, 2–3 sets of 8–10 reps). These develop anaerobic capacity and repeat sprint ability. (2) Small-sided games — reduce pitch size and player numbers (3v3, 4v4) to increase individual intensity. Heart rate should sustain at 85–95% HRmax. Include competitive scoring and consequences. (3) Sprint training — introduce maximal sprinting progressions. Week 3: flying sprints 10–20m (submaximal, 90%), 4–6 reps. Week 4: acceleration sprints 0–20m, near-maximal, 6–8 reps. Week 5: full-speed sprints with sport-specific starts (standing, rolling, reactive). Total sprint volume: increase from 30% to 75–90% of in-season levels by end of block. (4) Change-of-direction — sport-specific agility drills with progressive complexity. Start with planned agility (known patterns), progress to reactive agility (responding to visual/auditory cues). (5) Practice matches — introduce controlled scrimmages in week 4. First friendly/pre-season match in week 5. Initially limit match exposure to 45–60 minutes, building to full match duration. Strength training: transition from hypertrophy to max strength (4×4–6 at 80–90% 1RM, 2–3 sessions/week). Introduce power exercises: jump squats, Olympic lift derivatives, medicine ball throws. Reduce session frequency if match/training load creates recovery concerns. Key monitoring: track high-speed running and sprint distance — these should approach in-season averages by end of Block 2. Monitor hamstring and groin injury risk — these peak during pre-season Block 2 due to rapid sprint volume escalation (Ekstrand 2011). Ensure adequate progressive loading and prehab compliance.

## PERIODIZATION: In-Season Maintenance Training Structure
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT, READINESS]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

In-season training must maintain the physical qualities developed in pre-season while managing fatigue around competitions. The principle of "minimal effective dose" applies: what is the least training volume that prevents detraining while allowing competition recovery? Maintenance thresholds by quality: (1) Strength — minimum 1 session/week (can maintain for 8–12 weeks), ideally 2 sessions. 2–3 sets of 3–5 reps at 80–85% 1RM for major lifts (squat, deadlift, bench). Sessions should be <45 minutes. Schedule strength 48+ hours before competition. (2) Speed — 2 maximal sprint exposures per week, including matches. If only 1 match/week, include 1 dedicated speed session (6–8 sprints, 20–40m, full recovery). Sprint capacity declines rapidly without exposure — 7–10 days without maximal sprinting shows measurable speed loss. (3) Aerobic capacity — maintained through match play and training games. If extended period without matches (>10 days), add 1–2 aerobic maintenance sessions (20–30 min at 80–85% HRmax). (4) Power/plyometrics — 1–2 sessions/week, 40–60 ground contacts. Can be integrated into warm-ups (countermovement jumps, drop jumps, bounding). (5) Flexibility/mobility — daily maintenance, 10–15 minutes post-training. Focus on sport-specific areas: hamstrings and hip flexors for football, shoulders and thoracic spine for racket sports. In-season weekly structure (single game/match per week): Day 1 (MD+1): active recovery. Day 2 (MD+2): strength session + light technical. Day 3 (MD+3): highest training load day — tactical work, conditioning, game simulation. Day 4 (MD+4): moderate session — set pieces, technical work, speed maintenance. Day 5 (MD-2): light tactical, activation. Day 6 (MD-1): walkthrough, preparation. Day 7: MATCH. With 2 games/week: eliminate the high-load training day entirely. Focus only on recovery, activation, and tactical preparation between matches. Strength training moves to 1 brief session on the day with maximum gap between games.

## PERIODIZATION: In-Season Taper Protocol for Key Matches
rec_types: [READINESS]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Tapering — the systematic reduction of training load before important competition — can improve performance by 2–6% (Mujika & Padilla 2003). This is the difference between good and outstanding performance. Taper types and when to use them: (1) Step taper — sudden reduction of 40–60% of training volume. Simple but less effective. Use when time is limited (3–5 days before event). (2) Progressive (linear) taper — gradual reduction of volume over 1–3 weeks. Reduce volume by 20% in week 1, 40% in week 2, 60% in final days. Best for endurance events and tournament sports. (3) Exponential taper — rapid initial volume reduction that flattens out. Most evidence-supported method for team sports. Reduce volume by 40–60% while maintaining or slightly increasing intensity. Key taper principles: (1) Reduce VOLUME, not intensity — this is the most common error. Intensity must remain at or near competition level. Cut sets and reps, not the weight or speed. (2) Maintain training frequency — reducing session frequency by more than 20% during taper can impair coordination and "feel." Better to keep 4–5 shorter sessions than reduce to 2–3 longer sessions. (3) Duration — 7–14 days for major competition. Longer tapers (>14 days) risk detraining. Shorter tapers (3–5 days) for less important events. (4) Volume reduction specifics during a 2-week taper: Week minus 2: reduce total volume by 30–40%. Maintain 2 high-intensity sessions (sprints, game simulation). Maintain 1 strength session (reduce to 2×3–5 at 85%+). Week minus 1: reduce volume by 50–60%. 1 high-intensity session (short, sharp, 20–30 minutes). Remove strength training 3–5 days before competition. MD-2 and MD-1: activation only (15–20 min dynamic warm-up, 5–6 short sprints, set pieces/routine practice). Sleep, nutrition, and hydration optimization throughout. Psychological preparation: visualization, pre-performance routines, reducing external stressors. Youth-specific: U17 and younger may need shorter tapers (5–7 days) as their chronic training loads are lower and detraining occurs faster.

## PERIODIZATION: Off-Season Active Rest Phase (Weeks 1–2)
rec_types: [RECOVERY, WELLBEING]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

The off-season active rest phase (typically 2 weeks immediately following the final competition) serves critical physical and psychological recovery functions. This is NOT a period of complete inactivity — research shows that 2 weeks of complete rest causes: 5–10% reduction in VO2max, 5–10% strength loss, significant glycogen storage capacity reduction, and deterioration of coordination patterns (Mujika & Padilla 2000). However, training load should be dramatically reduced. Active rest prescription: (1) Structured training — ZERO sport-specific training sessions. No team practices, no technical drills, no tactical work. The athlete needs a mental break from their sport. (2) Physical activity — 3–4 sessions/week of enjoyable, non-sport-specific physical activity: recreational swimming, hiking, cycling, pickup games in OTHER sports, surfing, yoga, dance, martial arts. Intensity should be self-regulated and fun-focused. Duration 30–60 minutes. (3) Complete rest days — 2–3 per week with no structured physical activity. Sleep ad libitum (as much as desired, often 9–11 hours as sleep debt is repaid). (4) Physical maintenance — 1–2 very light strength sessions/week focusing on injury prevention exercises (prehab) and movement quality. Bodyweight exercises, mobility flows, foam rolling. No heavy loading. (5) Injury rehabilitation — this is the ideal time to address lingering injuries that were managed during the season. Physiotherapy, imaging, specialist consultations. Begin rehab protocols that would have interfered with competition. Psychological recovery goals: (1) Engage in non-sport identity activities — hobbies, social activities, family time. (2) Reflect on the season — successes, learnings, areas for development. Brief journaling, not formal review yet. (3) Set broad goals for next season — but avoid detailed planning, which activates performance anxiety. (4) Social media break from sport content — reduce comparison and performance pressure. Nutrition: maintain healthy eating habits but relax rigidity. Allow for social eating, treats, flexibility. Avoid dramatic weight gain (monitor — >3% body mass increase warrants attention). End of active rest: transition day assessment — body composition, basic movement screen, wellness check, goal-setting conversation.

## PERIODIZATION: Off-Season Development Phase (Weeks 3–8)
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

The off-season development phase (weeks 3–8 after season end) is the most valuable period for physical development without competition interference. This is when athletes make their biggest fitness gains. Training structure: (1) Strength development — this is the primary focus. 3–4 strength sessions/week (the only time of year this frequency is sustainable). Phase progression: weeks 3–4: anatomical adaptation / hypertrophy (3×10–12 at 65–75% 1RM), weeks 5–6: max strength (4×4–6 at 80–90% 1RM), weeks 7–8: power conversion (3×3–5 at 70–85% 1RM with explosive intent, plus Olympic lifts). Include both bilateral and unilateral exercises. Address identified weaknesses from end-of-season testing. (2) Speed development — 2 sessions/week focused on acceleration (10–30m) and maximum velocity (flying sprints). Off-season is ideal for speed development because CNS is fresh without competition fatigue. Include resisted sprinting (sleds, hills) and technique work (wall drills, wicket runs). (3) Aerobic base rebuilding — 2–3 sessions/week of aerobic conditioning. Long intervals (4×4 min at 90–95% HRmax), continuous runs (30–40 min at 75–80% HRmax), and game-based conditioning. Rebuild VO2max to pre-season entry target. (4) Sport-specific skills — reintroduce gradually in weeks 4–5 with individual technical sessions 2–3 times/week. Focus on skill development and weakness correction without tactical complexity. (5) Flexibility and mobility — daily work (15–20 min). Address chronic tightness areas identified during the season. This is the best time to make significant ROM improvements. Volume progression across the block: week 3 — 60% of peak in-season volume, week 4 — 70%, week 5 — 80%, week 6 — 90%, week 7 — 95%, week 8 — 85% (mini-taper before pre-season). Monthly testing schedule: test key metrics at weeks 4 and 8. Compare to end-of-season baselines. Target improvements: strength (10–20% gain in key lifts), speed (2–5% improvement in 10m and 30m), aerobic capacity (5–10% Yo-Yo IR1 improvement), body composition (increase lean mass, reduce fat mass to sport-specific targets).

## PERIODIZATION: Competition Period Peaking Strategy
rec_types: [READINESS, DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Peaking for the competition period requires strategic management of fitness, fatigue, and form — the fitness-fatigue model (Banister 1975). Performance = Fitness minus Fatigue. During the competition period, the goal is to maintain high fitness while reducing accumulated fatigue. Peaking strategy components: (1) Training load wave — do not maintain constant training load throughout the competition phase. Instead, use a 3:1 or 4:1 loading pattern: 3 weeks of normal competition-phase training followed by 1 "download" week with 30–40% reduced volume. This prevents chronic fatigue accumulation while maintaining fitness. (2) Strategic competition calendar — identify 2–3 "A" events (peak performance required), 4–6 "B" events (strong performance), and remaining "C" events (development/process focus). Only taper for "A" events. "B" events use mini-taper (3–5 days volume reduction). "C" events are trained through. (3) Fitness maintenance — during competition phase, physical quality maintenance requires: speed — 2 maximal sprint exposures/week (including competition), strength — 1–2 sessions/week at 80%+ 1RM (brief, 30 min), aerobic — maintained through competition and training games, power — 40–60 plyometric contacts/week integrated into warm-ups. (4) Freshness management — monitor accumulated fatigue through: daily wellness scores (rolling 7-day average), HRV trends (7-day rolling average compared to individual baseline), performance tests (CMJ weekly — decline >5% indicates fatigue), subjective readiness (RPE of training sessions trending higher at same intensity). (5) Mid-season peak — for sports with long seasons (20+ weeks), plan a deliberate mid-season break or reduced week coinciding with any league breaks. This prevents the common "mid-season slump" at weeks 10–14. (6) Late-season strategy — if competing for playoffs/finals, begin periodizing training 6–8 weeks out. Reduce overall volume by 10% per week while maintaining intensity. Final 2 weeks follow formal taper protocol. Communication: educate athletes that feeling "slightly undertrained" going into competition is the correct sensation — this is the taper effect.

## PERIODIZATION: Transition Periods — Detraining Prevention
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Transition periods between seasons or training blocks are high-risk for detraining — the partial or complete loss of training-induced adaptations. Understanding detraining timelines helps prescribe appropriate maintenance: (1) Aerobic capacity — VO2max declines 4–14% after 4 weeks of inactivity. The first 2 weeks see the largest drop (5–10%), primarily due to reduced blood plasma volume and stroke volume (Mujika & Padilla 2000). Maintenance requires minimum 2 aerobic sessions/week at >70% VO2max. (2) Strength — strength losses begin after 2–3 weeks of no training, with 5–10% loss by week 4. However, neural adaptations are retained longer than hypertrophy — "muscle memory" allows faster rebuilding. Maintenance: 1 session/week at 70–80% 1RM preserves strength for 8–12 weeks. (3) Speed — sprint ability decreases 2–5% after 4 weeks of detraining, primarily due to neural and coordination losses. Speed is the most "perishable" quality — requires at least 1 maximal sprint exposure/week to maintain. (4) Flexibility — ROM losses begin within 1–2 weeks without stretching. Daily maintenance stretching (even 5 minutes) prevents significant loss. (5) Sport-specific skills — complex motor patterns are retained well (months to years) but precision and timing deteriorate within 2–3 weeks without practice. Minimum 1 sport-specific session/week maintains skill sharpness. Transition period prescription: if the transition is planned (between seasons): follow the active rest + development phase protocol. If the transition is unplanned (injury, illness, unexpected break): week 1 — accept the rest, focus on recovery. Week 2 — begin light activity (walking, swimming) if medically cleared. Week 3 — reintroduce sport-specific activity at 50% intensity. Week 4 — build to 70%. Week 5–6 — return to normal training at 80–90%. For youth athletes: detraining occurs slightly faster than adults (smaller fitness reserve), making consistent year-round activity more important. However, variety of activity is more important than sport-specific training — playing different sports during transitions prevents detraining while providing psychological refreshment.

## WELLBEING: Burnout Warning Signs in Youth Athletes
rec_types: [WELLBEING, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Athlete burnout is a syndrome of emotional and physical exhaustion, reduced sense of accomplishment, and sport devaluation (Raedeke 1997). In youth sport, burnout rates range from 1–5% clinical and 15–35% subclinical (showing warning signs). Burnout differs from overtraining: overtraining is physiological fatigue that resolves with rest; burnout is a psychological condition that may persist even after rest. Early warning signs (act when 3+ present): (1) Emotional exhaustion — athlete appears "flat," lacks enthusiasm, dreads training (previously enjoyed). Unprompted statements like "I'm tired" or "I don't feel like going" become frequent. (2) Reduced performance despite adequate rest and training — performance plateau or decline that cannot be explained by physical factors. (3) Increased irritability and mood swings — disproportionate reactions to coaching feedback, conflict with teammates, uncharacteristic aggression or withdrawal. (4) Social withdrawal — avoiding teammates outside of training, declining social invitations, increasing isolation. (5) Physical complaints — frequent minor illnesses (colds, headaches, stomach problems), persistent muscle soreness that doesn't resolve with normal recovery, increased injury frequency. (6) Perfectionism escalation — excessive self-criticism, fear of making mistakes, inability to accept setbacks. (7) Sleep disturbance — difficulty falling asleep despite fatigue, waking during the night with performance-related thoughts. (8) Identity narrowing — defining self-worth entirely through sport performance. Risk factors: early single-sport specialization (2–3x higher risk), controlling coaching style, parental pressure exceeding athlete's own motivation, high training volume without adequate rest, lack of autonomy in training decisions. Intervention protocol: (1) Reduce training volume by 30–50% for 2–4 weeks. (2) Increase athlete autonomy — let them choose drills, set their own goals. (3) Reintroduce fun and social elements. (4) Explore non-sport identity activities. (5) If symptoms persist >4 weeks despite intervention, refer to sport psychologist. Prevention is far more effective than cure: regular check-ins about enjoyment, 2+ full rest days/week, annual rest period from primary sport, encouraging multi-dimensional identity.

## WELLBEING: Overtraining Syndrome vs Overreaching — Identification
rec_types: [READINESS, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Distinguishing between functional overreaching (FOR), non-functional overreaching (NFOR), and overtraining syndrome (OTS) is critical for youth athlete management (Meeusen 2013). Functional overreaching (FOR) — PLANNED short-term performance decline followed by supercompensation. Duration: 1–2 weeks of increased load, followed by recovery. Performance returns to above-baseline within 1–2 weeks of rest. This is a normal and desired part of periodized training. Signs: temporary fatigue, minor performance dip, but mood and sleep remain relatively normal. Non-functional overreaching (NFOR) — UNPLANNED performance decline that takes weeks to months to resolve. Duration: performance does not rebound within 2 weeks of reduced training. Signs: persistent fatigue despite adequate sleep, performance decline lasting 2–8 weeks, mood disturbance (increased depression, anxiety), disrupted sleep patterns, loss of appetite, increased susceptibility to illness, elevated resting heart rate (>5 bpm above individual baseline), decreased HRV (>15% below individual baseline). Overtraining syndrome (OTS) — severe maladaptation requiring months of recovery. Duration: performance decline lasting >2 months despite complete rest. Requires medical evaluation to rule out other conditions. Involves hormonal disruption (cortisol dysregulation, suppressed testosterone), immune suppression, and clinical-level psychological disturbance. Diagnostic framework for youth athletes: monitor these markers weekly: (1) Performance — if key test scores (CMJ, sprint time, sport skill accuracy) decline >5% for 2+ weeks, flag concern. (2) Resting HR — morning heart rate consistently >5 bpm above individual baseline. (3) HRV — 7-day rolling average declining trend >15% from baseline. (4) Wellness — daily wellness questionnaire: sleep quality, mood, fatigue, soreness, stress (each 1–5, total 5–25). Rolling average <15 for >5 days = flag. (5) Training response — RPE for a given training session trending upward (same workout feels harder). Management: FOR — continue planned overload then deload. NFOR — immediately reduce volume by 50% for 1–2 weeks, reassess. OTS — complete rest, medical referral, gradual return over months. Youth athletes are at higher OTS risk because: they often cannot recognize or articulate fatigue symptoms, they face cumulative stress from school, and coaches may underestimate total load.

## WELLBEING: Self-Talk and Performance Anxiety Management
rec_types: [WELLBEING]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Self-talk — the internal dialogue athletes maintain during training and competition — is one of the most powerful and accessible mental performance tools. Research shows that positive and instructional self-talk improves sport performance by 5–15% compared to negative self-talk (Hatzigeorgiadis 2011). Types of self-talk and applications: (1) Motivational self-talk — phrases that boost confidence and effort. Examples: "I've trained for this," "Stay strong," "One more rep." Best used during endurance efforts, when fatigue is high, during competitive pressure. (2) Instructional self-talk — technical cues that direct attention. Examples: "Head over the ball" (football), "Split step" (tennis), "Drive the knees" (sprinting). Best used during skill execution, learning new techniques, and correcting errors. (3) Negative self-talk — the enemy of performance. "I always miss these," "The opponent is better than me," "I can't do this." Negative self-talk increases cortisol, narrows attentional focus, and triggers "choking" under pressure. Intervention protocol: (1) Awareness — for 1 week, athletes log their self-talk during training using a simple diary (what was said, when, what triggered it). Most athletes are shocked by how much negative self-talk occurs unconsciously. (2) Thought stopping — when a negative thought is noticed, use a physical trigger (snap a wristband, clench fist) paired with the word "STOP" or "RESET." This interrupts the pattern. (3) Replacement — replace each identified negative thought with a pre-planned positive/instructional alternative. Write these on a card kept in the training bag. "I always miss under pressure" → "Stay calm, watch the ball, trust the process." (4) Practice — self-talk skills must be practiced in training before using in competition. Include self-talk cues in drill instructions. (5) Performance anxiety management — pre-competition anxiety is normal and can enhance performance (inverted-U theory). Techniques: box breathing (4 seconds inhale, 4 hold, 4 exhale, 4 hold — repeat 4 cycles), progressive muscle relaxation (tense each muscle group for 5s, release for 10s), centering (deep breath + focus word + let go of tension). Teach athletes the difference between facilitative anxiety ("I'm excited and ready") and debilitative anxiety ("I'm scared and will fail"). Reframing butterflies as readiness rather than fear is a powerful cognitive shift.

## WELLBEING: Goal Setting Frameworks for Youth Athletes
rec_types: [WELLBEING, DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Effective goal setting improves youth athlete motivation, self-confidence, and performance by 10–20% when done correctly (Burton & Naylor 2002). However, poorly implemented goal setting — overly focused on outcomes — can increase anxiety and reduce enjoyment. The goal setting framework for youth athletes should emphasize process over outcome. Three types of goals: (1) Outcome goals — results-focused (win the match, make the team, run a personal best). These provide direction and motivation but are partially outside the athlete's control (opponent strength, weather, officials). Limit to 1–2 outcome goals per season. (2) Performance goals — measurable personal benchmarks (improve 30m sprint by 0.2s, increase CMJ by 3cm, complete 10 consecutive free throws). These are within the athlete's control and provide clear feedback. Set 3–5 performance goals per training block. (3) Process goals — daily behaviors and habits (arrive 10 minutes early, complete full cool-down after every session, eat breakfast before school every day, practice visualization 5 minutes before bed). These are 100% within the athlete's control and build the foundation for outcome and performance goals. Set 5–8 process goals per month. SMART-ER framework: goals should be Specific, Measurable, Achievable (challenging but realistic — 50–70% confidence of achievement is the optimal difficulty level), Relevant (aligned with long-term development), Time-bound, Evaluated (reviewed at set intervals), and Revised (adjusted based on progress). Implementation: (1) Goal-setting session at start of each season — coach guides athlete through outcome → performance → process goal hierarchy. Document in a goal card. (2) Weekly check-in — 2-minute review of process goals (checklist format). (3) Monthly review — assess performance goals, adjust targets if needed (too easy = increase, too hard = reduce). (4) End-of-season reflection — evaluate all goal types, celebrate effort and improvement regardless of outcome goal achievement. Age-appropriate focus: U13 — primarily process goals (habits, effort, enjoyment). U15 — balanced process and performance goals. U17+ — full hierarchy with outcome goals introduced. Never punish for failing to achieve goals — instead, analyze why and adjust.

## WELLBEING: Team Dynamics and Individual Athlete Wellbeing
rec_types: [WELLBEING]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, basketball]
evidence_grade: B

Team dynamics significantly impact individual athlete wellbeing, performance, and retention. Research shows that athletes who report high team cohesion are 40% less likely to experience burnout and show 15–20% better adherence to training programs (Eys 2009). However, team environments can also be a source of significant stress, particularly for youth athletes navigating social hierarchies and identity formation. Positive team dynamics components: (1) Task cohesion — shared commitment to team goals and understanding of each member's role. Build through: clear role definition, team goal-setting sessions, celebrating collective achievements. (2) Social cohesion — interpersonal connections between team members. Build through: non-sport social activities (team meals, outings), peer mentoring programs (older athletes paired with younger), shared team rituals and traditions. (3) Psychological safety — each athlete feels safe to make mistakes, express opinions, and show vulnerability without ridicule. The single most important factor for healthy team culture. Built by coaches modeling acceptance of mistakes, praising effort over outcome, and addressing bullying immediately. Negative dynamics to monitor: (1) Clique formation — subgroups that exclude others. Intervene by rotating training partners, mixing groups for activities. (2) Bullying and hazing — zero tolerance. Affects 15–20% of youth athletes (Stirling 2011). Signs: withdrawal, reluctance to attend, declining performance, unexplained mood changes. (3) Unhealthy competition — when teammates undermine each other rather than push each other to improve. Reframe internal competition as "iron sharpens iron." (4) Playing time conflicts — the #1 source of team friction in youth sport. Address with transparent selection criteria communicated in advance. Individual wellbeing within the team context: schedule quarterly 1-on-1 conversations between coach and each athlete (not about performance — about enjoyment, relationships, stress). Use the Athlete Satisfaction Questionnaire or similar tool annually. Monitor individual engagement levels — declining engagement in team activities is an early burnout signal.

## WELLBEING: Parent-Athlete-Coach Communication Triangle
rec_types: [WELLBEING]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

The parent-athlete-coach triangle is the most influential relationship system in youth sport development. When all three parties communicate effectively, athlete development and wellbeing are optimized. When communication breaks down, athletes experience conflicting expectations, increased anxiety, and often dropout. Research indicates that parental behavior is the #1 factor in youth sport dropout, ahead of coaching quality and sport enjoyment (Fraser-Thomas 2008). Best practices for each relationship: (1) Coach → Parent communication: establish clear expectations at season start (parent meeting covering training philosophy, selection policy, communication channels, behavior expectations). Monthly updates on team progress and individual development (brief, 2–3 sentences per athlete). Address concerns promptly — unresolved parent frustration escalates rapidly. Set boundaries: no sideline coaching, 24-hour cooling-off period after competition before discussing concerns, communication through official channels only. (2) Coach → Athlete communication: age-appropriate feedback (U13: 80% positive, 20% constructive; U15: 70/30; U17+: 60/40). Feedback should be specific, behavioral, and timely. The "sandwich" method (positive-constructive-positive) remains effective for youth. Regular 1-on-1 goal-setting and review conversations. Empower athlete voice: ask what they want to work on, how they felt about training, what would help them improve. (3) Parent → Athlete interaction: the car ride home is the most impactful moment. Research shows the best thing parents can say after competition is: "I love watching you play" (nothing about performance). Avoid analyzing the game, questioning coaching decisions, or comparing with teammates. Support training adherence without controlling it — ask "Do you want to go to training?" not "You're going to training." As athletes mature (U17+), gradually shift from parent-led to athlete-led communication with coaches. Healthy triangle indicators: the athlete is the central figure in all decisions, parent and coach have respectful direct communication, and the athlete never feels caught between conflicting messages.
