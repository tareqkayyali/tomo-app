## READINESS: HRV as a Recovery Marker in Youth Athletes
rec_types: [READINESS, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) reflects parasympathetic nervous system activity and is the gold-standard non-invasive recovery marker. In youth athletes, HRV baseline should be established over 7-14 days of morning measurements. A drop >15% below individual baseline indicates parasympathetic suppression — the body is still recovering. Training intensity should be reduced to RPE 5-6 (zone 1-2) until HRV returns within 1 SD of baseline. Important: HRV is highly individual — absolute values are meaningless; only RELATIVE change from personal baseline matters. Day-to-day variation of 10-15% is normal. Trends over 3-5 days are more reliable than single readings.

## READINESS: RED Readiness Flag — When to Modify Training
rec_types: [READINESS]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

A RED readiness flag indicates multiple recovery signals are suppressed: elevated resting HR (>10% above baseline), depressed HRV, poor sleep quality (<6hrs or fragmented), high soreness (≥8/10), or pain reported. Under RED status: (1) No high-intensity training (>RPE 7), (2) No maximal-effort testing, (3) No plyometrics or heavy eccentric loading, (4) Light technical work, mobility, or active recovery ONLY. Attempting high-intensity training under RED increases injury risk by 2-3x (Gabbett 2016). The athlete should NOT feel guilty about modifying — recovery IS training. Return to full intensity only when readiness returns to AMBER or GREEN for 2 consecutive days.

## READINESS: Sleep Quality and Athletic Performance
rec_types: [READINESS, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Youth athletes require 8-10 hours of sleep for optimal recovery and adaptation. Sleep <7hrs reduces reaction time by 20%, increases injury risk by 1.7x, and impairs muscle protein synthesis by up to 30%. Sleep quality markers: (1) Sleep onset <20min, (2) No more than 1 awakening, (3) Time in bed efficiency >85%. For match-day preparation: target 9hrs the TWO nights before (not just the night before). Screen time cutoff 60min before bed. Post-training evening: light stretching + breathing exercises promote parasympathetic activation and faster sleep onset.

## LOAD_WARNING: ACWR and Injury Risk in Youth Sport
rec_types: [LOAD_WARNING]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

The Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (ACWR) compares recent load (7-day) to established fitness (28-day rolling average). The "sweet spot" for injury prevention is ACWR 0.80-1.30. Below 0.80 = undertrained (fitness declining). Above 1.30 = spike zone (injury risk increases exponentially). Above 1.50 = danger zone (2-4x injury risk). Important: Use the exponentially weighted moving average (EWMA) method, not simple rolling average, as it's more sensitive to recent changes. For youth athletes, be MORE conservative: sweet spot 0.85-1.20. Week-to-week load increases should not exceed 10% (acute spikes beyond this = injury risk).

## LOAD_WARNING: Training Load Spikes and Deload Protocols
rec_types: [LOAD_WARNING, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

When ACWR exceeds 1.30 or weekly load increases >15%, a structured deload is required. Deload options: (1) Volume reduction: maintain intensity, reduce total sets/reps/duration by 40-50% for 3-5 days. (2) Intensity reduction: maintain volume, reduce RPE to 5-6 for 3-5 days. (3) Active rest: low-intensity movement only (walking, swimming, yoga) for 2-3 days. The deload is NOT optional — it prevents overreaching from becoming overtraining syndrome (which can take 6-12 weeks to resolve vs 3-5 days of proactive deload). Post-deload: resume at 85-90% of pre-spike load and build gradually.

## RECOVERY: Active Recovery Protocols for Youth Athletes
rec_types: [RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Active recovery (light movement at HR zone 1, 50-60% max HR) within 24-48h post-match reduces delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) by 15-20% compared to complete rest. Protocol: 15-20min light cycling, swimming, or walking + 10-15min dynamic stretching + foam rolling on major muscle groups. Timing: Day+1 post-match or post-high-intensity session. Avoid: static stretching immediately post-exercise (reduces force output for 24hrs). Cold water immersion (10-12°C, 10-15min) has moderate evidence for reducing inflammation but may blunt training adaptations if used routinely — reserve for tournament/congested fixtures only.

## RECOVERY: Post-Match Recovery Timeline
rec_types: [RECOVERY, READINESS]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Match recovery follows a predictable timeline: 0-2hrs: refuel (1.0-1.2g carbohydrate/kg + 0.3g protein/kg within 30min). 2-12hrs: hydration (replace 150% of fluid lost). 12-24hrs: muscle damage peaks (CK levels highest). 24-48hrs: neuromuscular fatigue persists (reduce power output). 48-72hrs: full neuromuscular recovery for most youth athletes. Training implications: Day+1 = active recovery or rest ONLY. Day+2 = light technical, no high-intensity. Day+3 = return to normal training if readiness signals are GREEN. For congested fixture periods (2+ matches/week): prioritize sleep and nutrition over additional training.

## DEVELOPMENT: Speed Development Windows in Youth Athletes
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Speed development has two primary windows: (1) Pre-PHV (ages 7-12): focus on agility, coordination, change of direction, neural pathway development. Short sprints (<30m), multi-directional. Avoid heavy resistance training. (2) Post-PHV (ages 15+): neuromuscular maturity allows maximal sprint training, plyometrics, and strength-speed work. During PHV (Circa-PHV): CAUTION — rapid limb growth disrupts coordination. Focus on technique maintenance, not PBs. Reduce plyometric volume by 50%. Avoid heavy eccentric loading. Sprint mechanics work (A-skips, B-skips) is safe and beneficial. Key metric: 10m sprint time reflects acceleration ability (most sport-relevant). 30m reflects max velocity capacity.

## DEVELOPMENT: Strength Training Progression for Youth Athletes
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Youth strength training is safe and effective when properly progressed. Stages: (1) Movement competency (all ages): bodyweight patterns — squat, lunge, push-up, plank, hinge. Master form before adding load. (2) Load introduction (post-movement competency, typically U14+): light external load, 2-3 sets × 8-12 reps, 2x/week. Focus on rate of force development, not max load. (3) Strength building (post-PHV, typically U16+): progressive overload, 3-4 sets × 6-10 reps, compound lifts (squat, deadlift, bench). Weekly load increase ≤5%. Never train to absolute failure in youth athletes — leave 2-3 reps in reserve. Circa-PHV: reduce loading by 40%, maintain movement patterns, monitor for apophyseal pain (growth plate stress).

## DEVELOPMENT: Periodization for In-Season Youth Athletes
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT, LOAD_WARNING]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

In-season training must balance competition readiness with development. Weekly structure: Match-day minus 3 (MD-3): highest intensity training day (speed, power, tactical). MD-2: moderate intensity (technical, position-specific). MD-1: activation only (short, sharp, 30min max). MD+1: recovery/rest. MD+2: return to training (moderate). This creates a natural undulating load pattern that prevents ACWR spikes. In-season strength training: maintain 2x/week, reduce volume by 30% from off-season, maintain intensity. Priority: do NOT add training volume during congested periods — subtract. Quality > quantity.

## ACADEMIC: Managing Dual Load — School + Sport Stress
rec_types: [ACADEMIC]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

Academic and athletic stress share the same cortisol pathway. Exam stress + heavy training = compounded fatigue. Research shows: (1) cognitive fatigue before physical training reduces performance by 10-15%, (2) exam-week cortisol levels can match match-day levels, (3) sleep disruption from academic stress directly impairs physical recovery. Recommendations: During exam periods, reduce training volume by 30-40% but maintain some physical activity (reduces cortisol vs complete rest). Schedule study sessions 2+ hours before training. Use the "study-train-sleep" sequence (not "train-study") for optimal cognitive and physical outcomes. Academic load should be factored into total athlete load monitoring.

## ACADEMIC: Exam Period Training Modifications
rec_types: [ACADEMIC, READINESS]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

During exam periods (2+ exams within 7 days): reduce training to 60-70% of normal volume. Maintain 2-3 sessions/week but cap at 45-60min. Focus on enjoyment and stress relief rather than performance gains. Light aerobic activity (20-30min zone 1-2) before study sessions improves cognitive function by 15-20% (Hillman et al., 2008). Avoid: scheduling high-intensity training the evening before an exam (elevates cortisol, disrupts sleep). Study plan + training plan should be coordinated — Tomo's schedule engine accounts for exam proximity in the priority matrix. Post-exam: gradual return to full load over 5-7 days (avoid the "make up for lost time" spike).

## PHV: Training During Peak Height Velocity
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT, LOAD_WARNING]
phv_stages: [CIRCA]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Peak Height Velocity (PHV) is the period of fastest growth (typically 12-14 for boys, 10-12 for girls). During Circa-PHV: bones grow faster than muscles and tendons, creating vulnerability. Key risks: Osgood-Schlatter (knee), Sever's disease (heel), apophyseal avulsion fractures. Training modifications: (1) Reduce plyometric volume by 50%, (2) Reduce eccentric loading intensity, (3) No maximal 1RM testing, (4) Extra stretching for hip flexors, hamstrings, calves (tightest during growth), (5) Loading multiplier: 0.60× normal training load. Monitor: any new joint pain = stop activity and assess. Coordination may temporarily regress ("adolescent awkwardness") — this is NORMAL, not a skill deficit. Maintain technical practice but don't expect PBs.

## PHV: Growth-Related Injury Prevention
rec_types: [LOAD_WARNING]
phv_stages: [CIRCA]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Growth plate (physis) injuries account for 15-30% of youth sport injuries during PHV. Risk factors: rapid growth (>6cm in 6 months), high training volume, sport specialization. Prevention: (1) Nordic hamstring exercises 2x/week (reduce hamstring strain risk by 51%), (2) Balance and proprioception training (reduces ankle injury by 40%), (3) Hip and core stability work (reduces knee valgus), (4) Progressive return from any growth-related pain (minimum 2 pain-free days before return). Red flags requiring medical clearance: pain at rest, night pain, joint swelling, loss of range of motion. NEVER train through growth plate pain — the risk of permanent growth arrest is real.

## INJURY_PREVENTION: Pain Flagging and Training Decisions
rec_types: [READINESS, LOAD_WARNING]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

When an athlete reports pain in their check-in, follow this decision tree: (1) Pain at rest or night pain = DO NOT TRAIN, seek medical assessment within 48hrs. (2) Pain during warm-up that worsens = STOP SESSION, active recovery only. (3) Pain during warm-up that resolves = MODIFY — reduce intensity to RPE 5-6, avoid pain-provoking movements. (4) Pain only after training = MONITOR — log pain location and severity, modify next session if recurring. (5) Pain >3 days = REST + professional assessment. Critical: soreness ≠ pain. Soreness (bilateral, muscle belly, resolves with warm-up) is normal. Pain (unilateral, joint/tendon, worsens with activity) is a warning signal. Youth athletes often underreport pain — always ask specifically.

## INJURY_PREVENTION: Return-to-Play Criteria
rec_types: [READINESS, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Return-to-play after injury follows a graduated protocol: Stage 1 (pain-free daily activities), Stage 2 (light aerobic, HR <70% max), Stage 3 (sport-specific drills, no contact), Stage 4 (full training with contact), Stage 5 (match clearance). Minimum 2 days at each stage before progressing. Regression criteria: any return of pain = drop back one stage. For muscular injuries: strength must be within 10% of uninjured side. For joint injuries: full range of motion required before Stage 3. Psychological readiness matters — fear of re-injury is real and should be acknowledged, not dismissed. Use confidence scaling (1-10) alongside physical markers.

## MOTIVATION: Self-Determination Theory in Youth Sport
rec_types: [MOTIVATION]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: A

Intrinsic motivation (playing because you enjoy it) is the strongest predictor of long-term sport participation. Three basic psychological needs (Deci & Ryan): (1) Autonomy — choice in training decisions, goal-setting involvement. (2) Competence — appropriate challenge level, progress visibility, mastery experiences. (3) Relatedness — belonging, team connection, coach relationship. When these needs are met, athletes show higher effort, persistence, and well-being. When frustrated: burnout, dropout, anxiety. Coaching implications: offer choices within structure ("which drill set do you want to start with?"), celebrate process goals alongside outcome goals, never use training as punishment.

## MOTIVATION: Streak Psychology and Habit Formation
rec_types: [MOTIVATION]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

Consistency streaks leverage "loss aversion" — athletes are more motivated to maintain a streak than to start one. Research shows: 21 days creates a habit, 66 days makes it automatic (Lally et al., 2010). Design principles: (1) Make the minimum daily action EASY (a check-in counts), (2) Allow "freeze days" to prevent all-or-nothing abandonment, (3) Show streak milestones at 7, 14, 21, 30, 60, 90 days, (4) Social comparison (leaderboards) motivates competitive athletes but can demotivate lower performers — use with care. Key: the goal is ENGAGEMENT, not perfection. A 5-day streak with genuine effort > a 30-day streak of minimum-effort check-ins.

## NUTRITION: Youth Athlete Fueling Basics
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT, RECOVERY]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Youth athletes have higher metabolic rates per kg than adults. Daily energy needs: 2,500-4,000 kcal depending on age, size, and training load. Key principles: (1) Carbohydrate is the primary fuel for sport — 5-7g/kg/day for moderate training, 7-10g/kg/day for heavy training days. (2) Protein for recovery: 1.2-1.6g/kg/day, spread across 4+ meals/snacks (max 0.4g/kg per sitting). (3) Pre-training meal: 2-3hrs before, carb-rich, low fat. (4) During training >60min: 30-60g carbs/hr (sports drink or fruit). (5) Post-training: carbs + protein within 30-60min. NEVER restrict calories in growing athletes. Avoid supplements — food first. Creatine only post-PHV with professional guidance.

## NUTRITION: Match-Day and Competition Nutrition
rec_types: [DEVELOPMENT]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Match-day nutrition timeline: Night before: carb-loading meal (pasta, rice, potatoes) + good hydration. Morning of: familiar breakfast 3-4hrs before kickoff (toast, cereal, fruit, yogurt). 1hr before: light snack if needed (banana, energy bar). During: water + electrolytes every 15-20min. Halftime: 200-300ml fluid + small carb snack. Post-match: recovery meal within 60min (carbs + protein). Common mistakes: trying new foods on match day, caffeine use in U16 athletes (not recommended), skipping breakfast for early matches. Hydration check: urine should be pale yellow. Dark urine = inadequate hydration.

## CV_OPPORTUNITY: Talent Identification Benchmarks
rec_types: [CV_OPPORTUNITY]
phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football]
evidence_grade: B

Football talent identification uses multi-dimensional profiling. Physical benchmarks (academy-level U17-U19): 10m sprint <1.75s, 30m sprint <4.20s, CMJ >40cm, Yo-Yo IR1 >2,000m, repeated sprint ability (RSA) fatigue index <5%. These are REFERENCE points, not thresholds — technical ability, tactical intelligence, and psychological attributes are equally weighted. Position-specific emphasis: goalkeepers (reaction time, height, reach), centre-backs (aerial ability, sprint over 5m), midfielders (Yo-Yo IR1, passing accuracy), wingers (30m sprint, change of direction), strikers (CMJ, 10m acceleration). Showcase these metrics on your CV alongside game footage and coaching references.

## CV_OPPORTUNITY: Building an Athlete CV Profile

phv_stages: [POST]
age_groups: [U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

An effective athlete CV includes: (1) Bio (age, height, weight, position, dominant foot/hand), (2) Physical profile (key test results with percentile context — "10m sprint: 1.72s, P75 for age"), (3) Playing history (teams, competitions, representative honors), (4) Athletic development arc (improvement trajectory over 6-12 months), (5) Education status (for student-athletes), (6) Coach references (2 minimum). ## READINESS: Resting Heart Rate as a Recovery Indicator
rec_types: [READINESS]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U13, U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B+

Resting heart rate (RHR) is a simple but effective recovery marker when tracked consistently. Normal youth athlete RHR: 55-75 bpm (lower indicates better cardiovascular fitness). A rise of >5bpm above individual baseline suggests: inadequate recovery, illness onset, psychological stress, or overtraining. Measure: morning, before getting out of bed, after 2min of quiet lying. Factors that elevate RHR: caffeine, dehydration, poor sleep, illness, high training load. RHR combined with HRV provides a more complete picture of autonomic nervous system status. Trend over 7 days is more informative than single readings.

## RECOVERY: Mental Recovery and Psychological Load
rec_types: [RECOVERY, MOTIVATION]
phv_stages: [PRE, CIRCA, POST]
age_groups: [U15, U17, U19, ADULT]
sports: [football, padel, athletics, basketball, tennis]
evidence_grade: B

Physical recovery without mental recovery is incomplete. Psychological load sources: competition pressure, selection anxiety, academic stress, social media comparison, coach conflict. Signs of mental fatigue: decreased motivation, irritability, difficulty concentrating, increased perceived effort for same physical tasks. Recovery strategies: (1) Structured downtime (no sport-related content for set periods), (2) Mindfulness/breathing exercises (5-10min daily reduces cortisol by 20%), (3) Social connection outside sport context, (4) Sleep hygiene as priority. Youth athletes under 16 are particularly vulnerable to burnout from over-scheduling — ensure 1-2 completely free days per week.
